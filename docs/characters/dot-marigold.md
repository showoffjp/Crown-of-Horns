# 🛡️ Dot Marigold — *"The Feast That Has to End"*

> **Companion 8 of 8 (hidden).** A Baldur's Gate baker who buries everyone she loves and answers
> the grey cosmos by throwing a **forbidden party for the doomed.** Her arc is the game's answer
> **"joy"** — the road's one un-morose quest — and she is the **living refutation of the Deathless
> Garden**, the only soul in the cast who can name *why* a heaven with the dying edited out is a
> tomb. *"A feast that never ends isn't a feast. It's just a room you can't leave."*

---

## Identity & build
- **Full name:** Dorothea "Dot" Marigold, of the **Marigold ovens, Baldur's Gate** — three generations
  of bakers in the Heapside ward.
- **Class:** **Bard (College of Eloquence)** crossed with a homebrew *"Hearthkeeper"* kit — buffs,
  inspiration, and area heals that key off **food shared, songs sung, and lanterns lit** rather than
  spell slots; her toasts are mechanically *Bless*, her feast is a *Heroes' Feast.* Wields a long
  bread-peel like a quarterstaff and throws lit paper lanterns. **The only companion whose power
  *grows* the more the party celebrates between fights** — and *shrinks* if you let the road go grim.
- **Joins:** **lore-gated, hidden** — recruited Realm-side at **the Last Lantern-Feast** (the
  `lanternfeast` zone, off the Lamplit Quarter) **only if you defend the feast from Sister Vayle's
  church** rather than help shut it down. **Romance:** ✅ — warm, funny, the game's one *un-tragic*
  courtship (its tragedy is structural, not romantic: loving her means loving a thing that ends).

## Backstory
Dot baked for the living. Three generations of the Marigold ovens fed Heapside — bread on credit for
the families who couldn't pay, the first warm loaf of the morning left on the sill for whoever's
night had been worst. She was the woman who threw the **street's** parties: name-days, wakes,
midwinters, the lot. She believed, simply and without philosophy, that the point of being alive was
to **gather people into warm rooms and feed them while you still could.**

Then the **harvest-fever** came through the ward, the way it did every few years, and the year it took
her mother it also took her husband, and two of the children she'd helped raise, and the old man two
doors down she'd left bread for since she was a girl. She buried them. She baked for the wakes. And the
year after that, something *else* came through and took more, and she buried those too. Dot Marigold
spent her whole long life **burying everyone she loved on a rolling schedule** — and the thing that
should have broken her did the opposite. She came out the far side of all that grief holding one
hard-won, unbearable, **load-bearing** truth: that joy is precious *because* it ends. That you don't
throw the feast despite the dying. **You throw it because of the dying.** A loaf matters because it
goes stale. A name-day matters because there won't be infinite ones. The warm room is holy
*specifically* because everyone in it is, on some timetable, leaving.

She died old, in her own kitchen, mid-laugh, and crossed over carrying the recipe for the only thing
the afterlife of the discarded had never once had: **a party.**

## The wound
*She has had to learn, over and over, at funeral after funeral, exactly how short joy is — and the
lesson never stops costing.* Dot is not naïve; she is the opposite of naïve. Her cheer is not the
absence of grief but a **decision made on top of** more grief than anyone in the party except Ilfaeril.
Her wound is that **she is tired** — bone-tired of being the one who lights the lanterns, who insists
on the toast, who throws the feast while half the guests are dying, because *someone* has to and it's
always been her, and no one ever throws the feast for **Dot.** Her **need** is to be allowed, once, to
be the one who is *gathered* instead of the one who gathers — to let someone else light the lantern
for her.

## The secret — *the refutation of the Garden*
Dot is the only soul in the cast who can name, from the inside, **why the Deathless Garden is a tomb.**

The Unmade's masterstroke — the temptation that out-argues every companion — is a **real** heaven: the
Deathless Garden, where everyone you love is preserved, safe, smiling, *forever*, with the dying
quietly edited out. It is the ending that looks like winning. Garrow can't refute it (duty is *served*
by it). Naeve can't (it's the answer to every question). Maerin is its cruelest exhibit. The game
*needs* a soul who has stood in enough warm rooms full of the doomed to feel, in her gut, what's
**missing** from a feast that never ends — and that soul is Dot. Her secret is that she's **already
imagined the Garden**, every griever has, the fantasy of getting them all back and never burying
anyone again — and she knows precisely why it's a horror: *a feast that never ends isn't a feast. It's
a room you can't leave. The warmth was never in the gathering — it was in the gathering being **rare.**
Take away the ending and you don't get eternal joy. You get eternal Tuesday. You get the candle that
will not burn down, which is to say a candle that gives no light, because light **is** the burning.*
She is the antidote the player can carry into the last room. **If Dot is in your party at the Garden,
she gets the one speech that can break its spell** — not by hating it, but by *loving* it enough to
see the tomb inside the heaven.

## Want vs. need
- **Wants:** to keep the feast going — to be the one who insists on joy, lights the lantern, throws the
  party for the doomed, forever, because that's who she is and it's the only way she knows to fight.
- **Needs:** to let the feast, and herself, be **finite** — to stop being the eternal hostess long
  enough to be a *guest*; to grieve her own dead instead of baking through it; and to trust that joy
  she doesn't personally generate is still real. Her arc is the discovery that *she* is allowed an
  ending too — that the hearthkeeper is also, blessedly, perishable.

## Personal quest — *"The Last Lantern-Feast"* (woven Realm-side, then Acts III–V)
- **Ch. 1 — The Forbidden Feast (recruitment):** Sister Vayle's church has condemned Dot's lantern-feast
  as obscene — *the dead must not pretend they're still living; grief is the proper posture of a soul
  awaiting judgment.* You arrive as the church moves to put it out. **Defend the feast** (Persuasion /
  Intimidation / Performance — *join the toast*) and Dot joins, astonished that anyone came for *her*
  party. **Help shut it down** and she's lost as a companion — but you get a quietly devastating scene
  about a woman who's tired of being the only one who shows up.
- **Ch. 2 — The Empty Chair (Act III):** Dot keeps a place set at every camp meal for someone who isn't
  there. The party assumes it's sentiment. It isn't: it's **discipline** — a daily refusal to let the
  dead be edited out of the warmth, a chair that says *the gathering is for the ones who left too.*
  Her quest beat is the night she finally tells the party **who** the chair is for — all of them, the
  whole rolling decades of buried loved ones, named one by one — and asks the party, for the first
  time, to set a chair for **her** someday.
- **Ch. 3 — The Garden (Act IV–V):** the climax of her arc and one of the game's hinge scenes. In the
  Deathless Garden, Dot finds **her** dead — her mother, her husband, the children, the old man two
  doors down — preserved, smiling, *forever*, in an endless name-day that never ends. It is everything
  she ever wanted, and she stands in it and **weeps**, because she alone in the party can feel that the
  smiles are *frozen*, that the loaf on the table will never go stale and therefore was never bread,
  that her family has been turned into a **diorama of themselves.** Her choice — and yours, through her
  — is the whole game in one room: **walk out of the heaven** that has her whole family in it, because a
  feast that can't end isn't a feast; or **stay**, and let the lantern burn forever, which is to say
  give no light at all. *If she's romanced, the scene is unsurvivable in the best way.*

## Romance — *the un-tragic courtship with the tragic structure*
The game's one **warm, funny, low-angst** romance — and the cruelest, because its sorrow isn't in the
relationship, it's in the **premise.** Dot flirts by feeding you. The beats are comic: a disastrous
attempt to bake in a dead city with no working ovens; a toast that goes too honest; a midnight lantern
lit on a battlefield "because the day was awful and that's *exactly* when you light one." The turn is
quiet and gutting — the night she admits she's terrified to love you precisely *because* she knows how
this goes, that she has buried every single person she ever let into a warm room, and that loving a
Returned (a soul half-dissolved, walking toward a Wall) is signing up to bury one more. **To romance
Dot is to agree, knowingly, to be one more name on her rolling schedule — and to insist, with her,
that the warm room is worth it anyway.** Her best ending is the only one in the game where the lovers
*choose the finite over the eternal* with their eyes open.

## Approval & fates
- **Gains:** lighting the lantern when the day was worst; insisting on the toast, the camp meal, the
  small joy between catastrophes; mercy; honoring the dead by *celebrating* them, not just mourning
  them; letting yourself be glad. **Loses:** grimness-as-virtue; treating joy as frivolous or earned;
  "we'll celebrate when it's over" (it's never over); choosing any flavor of the static-forever; using
  the dead as fuel for the war instead of guests at the table.
- **Fates:**
  - **The feast goes on** — she returns to a freed Realm and throws lantern-feasts for the newly dead
    forever, the afterlife's first hostess, *and finally lets others throw one for her* (best);
  - **Burned out** — she keeps lighting the lanterns for everyone but herself until there's nothing
    left to light with, a hostess who never got gathered (the quiet tragedy);
  - **Preserved in the Deathless Garden** — the cruelest card the game can deal her: Dot Marigold,
    smiling, at an endless name-day, holding a loaf that will never go stale, **static**, the woman who
    knew better than anyone why this is a tomb, sealed inside it forever, *no longer able to feel that
    anything is wrong* — because in the Garden, nothing ends, including the lie.

## Key relationships
- **[The Unmade](the-unmade.md)** — her true antagonist, though they never fight: the Garden is the
  Unmade's love-letter to grievers like Dot, and she is the one argument against it that the Unmade
  cannot answer, because she **agrees** that the dying is unbearable and refuses the cure anyway.
- **[Sister Vayle](sister-vayle.md)** — the church that condemned her feast; grief-as-proper-posture
  against joy-as-defiance; the two women are the same wound (both have buried too many) facing opposite
  directions.
- **[Maerin](maerin.md)** — the girl preserved static in the Garden and the woman who can *name* why
  that's a horror; if both are present, Dot is the only one who can say out loud what's being done to
  Maerin, and it breaks the room.
- **[Ilfaeril](ilfaeril.md)** — the party's other soul carrying decades of grief; he answers it with
  penance, she answers it with a party, and their banter (the ten-thousand-year-old penitent and the
  baker who keeps trying to get him to *dance*) is the warmest thread in the camp.
- **[Sister Garrow](sister-garrow.md)** — Grave-cleric duty meets hearth-keeper joy; they argue about
  what the dead are *owed* — solemnity, or a good party thrown in their name — and both are right.

> Her answer to *what is a soul worth that no god ever claimed?* is **joy** — not joy as innocence, but
> joy as the hardest, most defiant thing a soul who has buried everyone can still choose: to light the
> lantern *because* the dark is coming, to throw the feast *because* it ends, and to know, as no one
> else in the cast knows, that a heaven without an ending is not a gift but the finest tomb ever built.
