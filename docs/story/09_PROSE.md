# ✍️ Prose Treatments — Key Scenes

> The marquee moments written as full prose — the tone target for writers, cinematics, and VO.
> Five pieces: the death (Prologue), the Wall (Prologue), the Deathless Garden (Act IV), the
> Mirror (Act V), and the Name (Act V finale). These are *scenes*, not summaries.

---

## I. The Death — *Prologue*

You do not see the blade. That is the first mercy and the last one: you do not see it coming, only
feel the cold of it arrive somewhere it should not be, a cold that is also, strangely, a kind of
*relief*, as though a question you'd carried your whole life without knowing it had finally been
answered. The cellar tilts. The Choir's lanterns smear into long gold streaks. Someone is chanting —
not in cruelty, you'll understand later, but in *grief*, which is worse. The soul-light comes out of
you the way breath comes out on a winter morning, visible, leaving, and you watch it go with the
mild, floating surprise of a person who has just realized they are no longer the one breathing.

The last thing you think, before the cellar lets you go, is not a prayer. You never learned one that
took. The last thing you think is the small, stupid, human thing — *I forgot to—* — and you never
finish it, and you never will, and somewhere very far below the floor of the world, something that
has been waiting ten thousand years for exactly this leans, at last, toward the warmth.

---

## II. The Wall — *Prologue*

The Fugue is grey, and the grey is not a color. It is the absence of the *argument* for color, the
flat patient nothing of a place that has given up expecting anything new. You are walking, though you
do not remember choosing to. Everyone here is walking. A long grey river of the recently dead,
shuffling toward a city that does not seem to get closer, and you are in it, and no one speaks,
because there is nothing here to say that has not already been said by everyone, forever.

And then you see the Wall.

It rings the city. It is made of *people.* That is the thing your mind refuses and refuses and
finally cannot refuse: the mortar between the stones is faces. Mouths. Open. Some still moving. Not
screaming — they are long past screaming — but *speaking*, the way a person speaks in sleep, small
fragments of names and weather and someone please and I only ever, packed in, pressed flat, dissolving
so slowly that slowness becomes its own eternity. The godless dead. The ones no one came for. And
they are not being *punished*, that is the obscenity of it, there is no torturer, no fire, no demon
with a list — there is only the patient architecture of a rule no one will admit they made,
unmaking them one unremembered syllable at a time.

You walk the Wall the way you'd walk a memorial, because that is what your mind does to survive it:
it makes the atrocity into a monument. And then, near the end of the long grey curve, you find the
niche. It is empty. It is the only empty one. And carved above it, half-formed, in a hand you almost
recognize, is a space the exact shape of your name.

You have time to feel the floor of your understanding give way. You have time to think: *that's not
possible, I only just—*

And then the hand reaches in — not Kelemvor's hand, not a god's, something older and colder and
*sadder* than any god — and it closes around the place where your soul has begun, already, to
soften toward the mortar. And it *pulls.*

The grey shatters like a held breath. You come back screaming into a corpse that is learning, with
great reluctance, to be a person again, and the Choir's herald is staring at you with a face gone
white as the Wall, whispering the same three words over and over, as though they were the worst
thing he knows:

*"You came back."*

## III. The Deathless Garden — *Act IV*

The Last Returned does not attack you here. It opens a door.

On the other side of the door is a morning. *The* morning — golden, particular, the light at exactly
the angle that means late spring on the Sword Coast, the angle that means *nothing bad has happened
yet today.* And in the morning is a garden, and in the garden are the dead.

Saerin is there, bent over a map, arguing cheerfully with a mountain. Aldric is there, *young*,
unbroken, no grey in his hair or his soul, laughing at something. Maerin is there — sixteen, whole,
luminous in the ordinary way that a living girl is luminous, not the fading half-light of the Wall —
chasing something through the hedges, calling back over her shoulder a joke you can almost hear.
Everyone is there. Everyone the Wall ever took. The Crown-War damned, lounging in the grass like
they were never enemies. The Netherese dead, looking up at a sky their city did not fall out of. The
small forgotten everyones of ten thousand years, *saved*, every one, fed and warm and laughing in the
late spring light.

*This is what you make,* the Last Returned says, beside you, in your own voice, very gently. *This is
what "yes" builds. Look at her. Look at Maerin. Alive. Forever. Tell me the price is too high. Look at
her face and tell me.*

And you look. And it is the most beautiful thing you have ever seen, and you want it so badly that the
wanting is a physical pain, a hand closing around the place where your soul softens, and you take one
step toward the door—

—and you notice the smile.

Maerin is laughing, mid-chase, her face caught in a grin of pure delight. And then the moment loops.
And she is laughing, mid-chase, the same grin, the exact same grin, the *identical* photons in the
identical light, and the joke she's calling back is the same joke, word for word, and Saerin makes the
same cheerful argument with the same mountain, and Aldric laughs at the same unheard thing, and the
late spring morning that means *nothing bad has happened yet today* will never, ever become afternoon,
because afternoon is change, and change is loss, and loss has been abolished, and so has growth, and so
has surprise, and so has the next joke, and so has tomorrow.

They are not living forever. They are the same instant of living, photographed and pinned and lit and
*held*, a held breath that will never exhale, a girl frozen one syllable short of the end of her
sentence, smiling, smiling, smiling, while the man who loves her sits across the garden and is
permitted, forever, to never have to grieve.

*You see it now,* the Last Returned says, and for the first time its certainty cracks, just slightly,
just enough to show you the centuries of horror underneath. *I built them a heaven. I built it out of
love. And it took me a hundred years to understand that I had stopped them. That mercy, given without
limit, is just a very kind, very permanent—*

It cannot say the word. It has been here too long to say the word. So you say it, looking at Maerin's
frozen smile, and it is the cruelest and most necessary word in the game:

*"Stop."*

The door closes. The golden morning is gone. And you understand, finally, what you are fighting — not
a monster, not a villain, but a *mercy* so total it forgot that a life needs an ending the way a
sentence needs a last word, the way a song needs a final note, the way love — real love, the kind that
can be lost — needs the terrible, holy possibility of grief.

## IV. The Mirror — *Act V*

It takes off its hood, in the Court of the Dead, and it is you.

Not a twisted you. Not a corrupted, glowing-eyed, obviously-evil you. *You* — older, yes, the way
ten thousand years are older, but unmistakably, unbearably *you*: your stance, your scars, the way you
hold your weapon, the small unconscious gesture you make when you're afraid and pretending not to be.
It makes the gesture now. It is afraid. Of course it is. It remembers being afraid here, the last
time, and the time before.

*I'm not going to insult you by pretending this is a fight you can win,* it says, and its voice is
your voice, gentle, exhausted, kind. *You can't beat me. I'm you, plus everything you're about to
learn. Every move you've got, I've already made. Every clever thing you're about to try, I tried, and
it worked, and it brought me here. To this room. To this hood. To this.*

It gestures at the throne behind it. At the frozen garden you can still feel, just past the edge of
the world.

*I came back along the whole loop to find you,* it says, *not to kill you. To save you the centuries.
You're going to say yes — I know you are, because I did, because we're good, because we cannot look at
the Wall and choose the rules over the children. And then you're going to spend a hundred years
learning what I learned. So let me give you that hundred years back. Let me make it quick. Say yes now,
and skip the part where you find out.*

And here is the thing the Last Returned does not understand, the thing it ground out of itself across
ten millennia of certainty, the one variable its perfect memory cannot hold: it remembers every move
you have ever made. It does not remember the moves you have *not* made, because you have never made
them, in any cycle, ever. It has perfect knowledge of your past. It has *no* knowledge of your refusal,
because refusal has never happened. You are about to do the only thing in ten thousand years that is
not already a memory.

You lower your weapon.

It blinks. That, it did not expect. That is not in the loop.

*"I'm not going to fight you,"* you say. *"And I'm not going to become you. I'm going to do the thing
neither of us has ever done."*

And for one instant — one single, unrepeatable instant — the Last Returned's face does something its
face has not done in an age. It *hopes.* And then it weeps, because it remembers, now, the thing it
expelled long ago, the small mournful fragment of itself that drifted back down the loop to find you
first: the **Mournlight**, the piece of it that always, secretly, wanted exactly this.

*"...Oh,"* it says, in your voice, very quietly. *"Oh. You found a way. You actually—"*

And it cannot finish, because you have already turned to the Wall, and the name is already rising in
your throat, the name you have spent the whole game forgetting on purpose, and you are about to say it
out loud.

---

## V. The Name — *Act V finale*

You remember it the way you remember a thing you have always known and spent your whole life pretending
you didn't.

It is not a grand name. It is not a god's name or a hero's name. It is small, and plain, and *yours* —
the name a parent gave a child ten thousand years ago in a village whose own name is dust, a child who
grew up godless under an open sky, who died young of an ordinary thing, who believed in nothing and so
was taken by a vote and a verdict and a Wall, who became the first discarded soul, who was pulled back
to be a key, who became the grief that pulls back the key, who has worn a thousand faces and a thousand
names since and forgotten this first one in every single cycle because remembering it is the one thing
that ends the game.

You say it.

You say your true name, out loud, in the Court of the Dead, to the Wall and the Unmade and the weeping
mirror of your future and the god who reads the last page and finds it, at last, *surprising* — and the
saying of it does what nothing in ten thousand years has done. It does not break the Wall. It does not
seize the Crown. It does not freeze the world into a smiling tomb.

It lets you grieve.

The Unmade — the vast grey sum of everyone the cosmos threw away, made of a million unremembered
syllables, all of them *you* — hears its own first name spoken for the first time since the first
verdict, spoken *willingly*, spoken with love, by the only soul that could, and the endless circle of
its sorrow finds, at the very last, the one thing it never had: an outside. A witness. A *name.* A
place to set the grief down.

And depending on the road you walked — depending on Jergal's keyhole and Maerin's choice and Garrow's
rite and the thousand small mercies and cruelties of a hundred hours of play — you choose what the
saying of the name *does.* You free the dead and damn the living, and become the smiling thing in the
garden. You keep the Wall, and carry the weight of having guarded it. You take up Jergal's pen and the
unbearable, holy, tedious work of judging every discarded soul by hand, forever, calling each one by a
name, starting with your own. You take the Crown and become the next thing that must be undone. Or you
do the smallest and largest thing of all: you step into the empty niche on the Wall that has had your
name on it since before the world was young — not to dissolve, this time, but to *rest* — and you let
the loop, finally, for the first time, for all of you, for the next one who would otherwise have to read
the note again —

*end.*

> *"You came back. They never come back."*
>
> The whole game was the discovery of why you always do — and what it costs, just once, to stay gone.
