# 📖 The Complete Synopsis

> **Total spoilers.** This is the entire game, front to back, every twist in the order the
> player experiences it. Prose treatment — the through-line a novelist or showrunner would work
> from. Mechanics and chapter logistics live in `03_ACTS.md`; this is the *story*.

---

## The world before you

It is the Year of the Iron Dwarf's Vow — **1486 DR**, the late days of the Second Sundering, on
the **Sword Coast**. Baldur's Gate is fat with trade and rot. To the north the **Fields of the
Dead** keep their centuries of war-corpses. And beneath all of it, in the **Fugue Plane** where
the dead are sorted, the **Wall of the Faithless** still stands — a living mortar of every soul
that died believing in no god, packed in and slowly dissolved to nothing. It is older than the
god who guards it. No one remembers building it. It simply *is.*

This is the lie the whole story takes apart: that *nothing* is a real place a soul can be sent.

---

## PROLOGUE — *What the Dead Owe*

You are nobody in particular — a Returned-to-be living an ordinary life in the Gate's lower
wards — until the night the harvest takes you. A cult that calls itself the **Choir of the
Unwalled** is bleeding soul-light out of the living in the undercellars beneath the city, and
you are in the wrong cellar. You **die.**

And you *wake* in the Fugue Plane. You see the City of Judgment. You see the Wall — and you see,
mortared into it with a thousand others, a space with **your name on it,** waiting. A voice like
a cathedral full of weeping speaks one word you will spend the whole game trying to remember.
And then something reaches in and tears you *back out.*

You return to your corpse in the cellar as it draws breath again. The Choir's herald — a masked
zealot — stares at you in something close to terror. *"You came back. They never come back."*
You are **Returned**: you see the freshly dead lingering at the edges of rooms, and there is a
cold crack down the middle of your soul that aches when the world is thin. You fight your way
out of the **Cinderhaunt** (the harvest-site, now boiling with the **Unbound** — souls the
harvest tore loose, come back wrong) and stagger into the city, marked, hunted, and alive.

> *The hook the player feels:* you have already seen the worst thing the cosmos does to the
> forgotten — and you have already had your own name reserved on it.

## ACT I — *The Gilded Gate* (Baldur's Gate)

Three powers want you, and the Crown of Horns they're all chasing.

**Aldric Morn** wants you because the Returned are, the Choir believes, a *key.* You meet him
not as a monster but as a tired, gentle, devastating old man who brings you tea and tells you
the truth: he was the Sword Coast's finest **Doomguide of Kelemvor** for forty years, until his
godless daughter **Maerin** died of plague and he followed a vision into the Fugue and watched
his own god's law wall her in to dissolve. He begged. Kelemvor — bound by a law older than
himself — refused. So Aldric will *become* the god of death and tear the Wall down with his
hands. To do it he needs the **Crown of Horns** (which still holds the residual divinity of the
dead god **Myrkul**) and enough soul-light to ignite it. The horror is not that he's mad. The
horror is that **he's right about the Wall**, and his followers — grieving families, doubting
Doomguides, the Faithless who fear their own end — *volunteered.*

**High Doomguide Sister Vayle** wants you in a cell. She leads Kelemvor's church on the coast,
hunts Aldric as an apostate, and will do monstrous things to keep the Crown buried — because the
sealed church archives told her what happens *every* time a mortal tries to reform death. She is
the boot you instinctively hate and, infuriatingly, the only person telling a kind of truth.

**The Harpers** want you handled. They send **Roen Alleywind**, a charming fixer, to recruit and
quietly *contain* you — a soul with a hole in it is a security risk. (Roen will stop reporting.)

You gather your first companions — **Sister Garrow**, the Doomguide who *didn't* follow Aldric;
**Roen**; the acid-tongued tiefling warlock **Varra** — and maneuver through the Gate's dukes,
Flaming Fist, and thieves toward the only place that knows where the Crown lies: **Candlekeep.**
At Act's climax, Vayle **burns a harvest-village** to deny you the trail — you watch her "lesser
evil" entire — and as you touch the Crown's psychic wake for the first time, **the world skips.**
For three heartbeats you are standing somewhere else, *somewhen* else, in a city in the sky. Then
you're back, nosebleeding, and no one else felt it. The crack in your soul has started to *open.*

> *First twist landed:* the easy villain is right, his cult is sincere, and your "second life"
> comes with a door in it that opens onto somewhere that should not exist.

---

## ACT II — *The Book and the Wall* (Candlekeep → the Fugue Plane)

You buy your way into **Candlekeep** with a book it doesn't have (a quest in itself), and in its
deepest stacks you find **the turn**: two truths, side by side.

First — the Crown of Horns is **not a tool.** It is **Myrkul**, patient and parasitic, *letting*
Aldric do the work, because a sympathetic martyr is the perfect vehicle to walk the cruelest of
the old dead-gods back into the City of Judgment and onto the throne. Aldric the abolitionist is
a mask Myrkul is wearing. Aldric does not know.

Second — and worse — **you are the crack in the ritual.** The reason the harvest could pull you
back is that your soul was *already* fractured along a seam the Choir's magic only widened. You
are not just a key. You are a *fault line in reality,* and everyone who understands what that
means has been very careful not to tell you.

To prove it, you do the impossible: you descend into the **Fugue Plane** itself, into the City of
Judgment, and you walk the Wall of the Faithless — and you **pull Maerin out of it.** Aldric's
daughter, half-dissolved, furious, luminous, and entirely her own person. She does not want to be
her father's excuse. She does not want to be *yours.* She is the moral center of the back half of
the game: the actual stake, made flesh, with opinions.

Here the companion quests detonate (Garrow's mentor among the Unbound; Varra's patron circling the
Crown; Roen's Harper handlers ordering your death). And here, at the lip of the Wall, **the Unmade
speaks to you directly for the first time** — not as a monster, but as a million grieving voices in
chorus, the condensed *nothing* of every dissolved Faithless soul across ten thousand years, awake
and sorrowing. It does not want power. It wants the cruelty that made it to **have never happened.**
And it knows how: *unwrite the law of the Faithless at its source — walk back to the beginning and
pull the first thread.* You are the only soul who can walk that road, because you are the crack.

It reaches in. The world doesn't skip this time. It **tears.** And you fall out of your own century.

> *The midpoint reveal:* the relic is a god; you are a wound; the real antagonist is grief itself,
> made vast and sapient and *correct.* The game stops being about a city and starts being about
> the structure of reality.

## ACT III — *The First Apocalypses* (Netheril, −339 DR → the Crown Wars, ~−10,000 DR)

The time-travel act. The Unmade scatters you through the hinge-points where death, magic, and time
were set or shattered, and at each one it is trying to *edit history* — and at each one, **someone
has already been there before you, wearing your face.**

**Netheril, −339 DR — Karsus's Folly.** You arrive in a **flying city** in the instant a mortal
arcanist named Karsus casts the most ambitious spell in history: an attempt to seize the goddess
of magic herself and *become* the god of the Weave. It is the original sin the whole game rhymes
with — Aldric's crime, ten thousand years early. You watch the goddess die to stop him; you watch
the Weave fail; you feel every spell in the world wink out at once — and you are standing in a city
that flies *by magic* the instant magic stops. The city falls out of the sky. In the screaming ruin
you pull a survivor from the rubble: **Naeve of the Seventh Enclave**, an arcanist who helped *build*
the theorem Karsus weaponized — a mother of the first apocalypse, and a woman who has read enough
forbidden mathematics to suspect the Unmade is real. She joins you. She also recognizes the seam in
your soul, because she has seen its signature once before: in the margins of a proof, in a hand she
swears is *yours,* written centuries before you were born.

**The Crown Wars, ~−10,000 DR.** You walk the elven civil wars that birthed the drow and the first
mass-damnations — the era when the **law of the Faithless was first enforced** as a *choice*, a
verdict elven lords handed down on their enemies' souls, before it calcified into cosmic law. You
recruit **Ilfaeril**, an elf paladin who survived that first damnation — and who, you learn, was one
of the lords who *voted for it.* He has spent ten thousand years at the edges of the Fugue trying to
atone. He is living proof of the game's most dangerous idea: **the Wall is not physics. It is a
decision someone made, and decisions can be unmade.** The Unmade adores him. So should you fear it.

And everywhere, the **Last Returned** has been first. An era-companion remembers *you* doing things
you have not done. A door is already open that you were going to open. A man you were going to kill
is already dead, killed kindly, by someone who looked like you and knew exactly where his heart was.
At Act III's climax you meet your hunter face to face — a pale, patient figure who lowers its hood
and wears **your face, aged by centuries of certainty** — and makes its case not with a blade but
with a memory: *it remembers being you,* remembers every choice you are about to make, and it is here
not to kill you but to **spare you the long road** to the throne it already sits on. And the Unmade,
gently, makes its offer at last: *help me pull the first thread, and your daughter — Maerin — and
every Faithless soul in every age are saved. I ask only what was already taken from you. An ending.*

> *Act III's gut-punch:* your "destiny" is **déjà vu of another self's choices.** You have been the
> villain of your own story for centuries, in a timeline you don't remember living. The future is not
> open. It is a *memory.*

## ACT IV — *Where the Ink Runs* (the Time of Troubles, 1358 DR → the Spellplague, 1385 DR)

Reality at its thinnest. You witness the events that made the very relic you carry.

**The Time of Troubles, 1358 DR.** The gods are cast down to walk Faerûn as mortals; they bleed in
the streets; Myrkul **dies** above Waterdeep. You can be present at the precise moment his skull is
taken up and the **Crown of Horns is forged** from it — and you understand, finally, what is riding
in your pack. The Crown-Voice (Myrkul) has been a sly, reasonable "companion" since Act II; here it
drops the geniality and shows you the throne it actually wants.

**The Spellplague, 1385 DR.** The goddess of magic is murdered a *second* time; the Weave collapses;
**blue fire** runs across the world and two worlds bleed together and *causality becomes optional.*
This is where the Unmade nearly wins — where the timeline is so wounded that a single pulled thread
could unravel everything. And it is where the **Last Returned** makes its real argument: it stops
fighting you and simply **shows you its world** — the timeline where you said *yes.* The Wall is gone.
Every Faithless soul, saved. Maerin, alive. Aldric, at peace. And it is **beautiful, and deathless,
and utterly, perfectly still** — a world where nothing ever ends, and so nothing ever changes, ever
grows, ever *means.* A paradise that is also a held breath that never exhales. *This is what mercy
costs,* it tells you, *and I would still pay it again. So will you.*

Here a companion can **die for good,** or **leave** — the choices have weight and the game stops
protecting you from consequence. Romances reach their breaking or their blooming. And you must decide,
before the convergence, **what kind of fixed point you intend to be.**

> *Act IV's devastation:* the villain shows you heaven and lets you understand exactly why it's hell.
> The "good ending" the Unmade offers is real, achievable, and damning. There is no strawman left to
> hate — only a grief you share and a price you can't unsee.

---

## ACT V — *The Crown of Horns* (the City of Judgment — all eras converging)

Every thread arrives at once at the **Court of the Dead.** Aldric, with the Crown and a full Choir.
Myrkul, ready to shed him. The Unmade, vast beneath the floor of the world. Vayle, with the church's
last desperate army. **Jergal**, the bored eldest scribe of the dead, who gave up godhood from sheer
weariness ages ago and has watched the Unmade grow for ten millennia, saying nothing, *curious how it
ends.* And the **Last Returned**, come to close the loop.

And here, if you have followed the deepest thread — the **Nameless** path, the forgotten word from the
prologue, the hand in Naeve's proof, the space on the Wall with your name — you learn the final, sleep-
stealing truth:

**The Unmade has no beginning.** It is a closed loop with no first cause. Because the *first* Faithless
soul it ever pulled back — ten thousand years ago, before Netheril, before the Crown Wars, at the very
forging of the law of the Faithless — was **you.** The word it spoke over your corpse in the prologue
was **your own true name,** the one you've spent the whole game failing to remember. You are not the
Unmade's key. You are not even its victim. **You are its origin.** Every Returned, in every age, has
been you, pulled back, choosing, becoming the Last Returned, seeding the grief that makes the Unmade
that pulls back the first soul that is *you.* The serpent has always been eating its own tail. There was
never a beginning. There has only ever been the loop, and the loop is *you,* grieving yourself across
all of time.

That is the horror. And the choice that ends the game is the only thing in all of history that the loop
cannot predict — because it is the one thing you have never done before. (See `04_ENDINGS.md`.)

## The emotional arc, compressed
Ordinary life → violent death → the terror of the Wall → a mystery in a great city → a mentor who is a
monster who is *right* → a daughter pulled from oblivion → the discovery that you are the wound → the
fall out of your own time → flying cities and damned elves and a hunter with your face → heaven revealed
as a tomb → and finally a throne room at the end of all things where you learn you have been the author
of your own grief since before the world was young — and you are handed the one pen the loop never had:
the power to write *the end.*

> *"You came back. They never come back."*
> The whole game is the discovery of why **you** always do — and what it would cost, for once, to stay
> gone.
