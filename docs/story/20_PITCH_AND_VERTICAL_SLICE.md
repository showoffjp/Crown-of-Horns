# 🚀 The Pitch & The Vertical Slice

> The document that turns a 45,000-word dream into a fundable, buildable plan. Part I is the pitch (the
> Kickstarter / publisher one-pager). Part II is the vertical-slice spec (the one playable hour that
> proves the whole game). Part III is honest scope, budget tiers, and risk. Write the slice; *then*
> raise the money. Never the other way around.

---

## PART I — THE PITCH

### Logline
> *A dead soul, dragged back from oblivion by the grief of everyone the gods threw away, must walk
> backward through the apocalypses of history to stop a mercy that would unmake the world — and
> discovers the monster behind it all has been wearing their own face since before time began.*

### The hook (the one paragraph that sells it)
**The Crown of Horns** is a party-based isometric CRPG in the tradition of Baldur's Gate II and
Planescape: Torment, built on D&D 5e. You die in the first hour — and are pulled back by a grieving
cosmic absence made of every soul the afterlife ever discarded. To stop it, you travel through the
literal hinge-points of the Forgotten Realms' history, gathering companions across ten thousand years,
chasing a hooded hunter who wears your own face. It's a tactical 5e RPG with the soul of a Greek
tragedy, and a central twist designed to make players put the controller down and stare at the wall.

### Why it's different (the 5 unique selling points)
1. **Mercy is the trap.** Most RPGs make cruelty the dark path. Here, your *compassion* is the engine
   of the apocalypse — a complicity twist no major RPG has done.
2. **The villain is you, fairly foreshadowed.** A KOTOR-grade identity twist, planted from hour one so
   it passes the replay test (see the Foreshadowing Ledger, `13` §I).
3. **Time travel as both story and structure.** Each era is a self-contained module (hub + dungeons +
   companions) — epic *and* productionally modular.
4. **Combat that asks moral questions.** Lay vs. Shatter, bosses you beat by *not* fighting — the
   tactics *are* the theme (`19`).
5. **An ending that costs you, not the avatar.** A once-only meta-beat (`13` §VI) in the rarest ending.

### Comparables (the "for fans of")
Baldur's Gate II (companions, reactivity, a villain who's *right*) · Planescape: Torment (identity,
mortality, philosophy-as-gameplay) · Disco Elysium (ideas as characters, melancholy) · Hades (readable,
beautiful combat staging) · NieR (a twist that touches the player).

### Tone & promise
Melancholy-warm, humane, devastating. Sorrow with warmth, never nihilism. We promise: deep mystery,
esoteric lore, complex philosophy, three slow-burn romances, real tragedy and real triumph, and **one
"HOLY SHIT, did that just happen?!" moment you will tell your friends about.**

### The art & sound in one line
*Warmth = mortality* (color), and one unfinished six-note phrase (*The Niche*) hidden in every theme
until it finally resolves on your true name. (Full bible: `18`.)

## PART II — THE VERTICAL SLICE (the one hour that proves the game)

> The single most important thing to build first. ~60 minutes, the **Prologue + the opening of Act I**,
> containing *every system the full game uses, in miniature.* If this hour is gripping, the game works.
> If it isn't, no amount of content saves it. Build this *before* raising money or writing Act III.

### What's in the slice (the checklist)
| System | Slice content | Proves |
|---|---|---|
| **Character creation** | full race/class/background/point-buy | the on-ramp, and that *your build matters to the twist* (the Mirror is your face) |
| **The Death + the Wall** | the Prologue: die, walk the Wall, find your niche, get pulled back | the emotional engine & the central mystery — in the first 10 minutes |
| **Tactical 5e combat** | the Cinderhaunt escape + the Maw mini-boss | turn-based depth, conditions, AoE, **Lay vs. Shatter** |
| **Exploration & a reactive hub** | a slice of Baldur's Gate — walk, talk, take a quest | the world loop, the living city |
| **Branching dialogue w/ stakes** | **"Tea with a Heretic"** (Aldric) — fully voiced if possible | the writing bar; a villain who's *right* |
| **A companion + the start of a bond** | recruit Garrow *or* Roen; first approval beats | party depth, the promise of romance |
| **One foreshadow seed, planted** | the `prior_returned` clue or the niche detail | that the game is *fair* — the replay-test promise |
| **The first skip** | the Act I cliffhanger: time comes loose for 3 seconds | the scope reveal — "this is bigger than a city" |

### The slice's emotional arc (the 60-minute shape)
Create your hero (5 min) → an ordinary moment, then **death** (10 min) → the silent walk along the Wall,
your name on it (5 min) → pulled back, the Cinderhaunt escape + the Maw, first Lay/Shatter (15 min) →
into Baldur's Gate, meet Garrow, take the harvest-trail quest (10 min) → **tea with Aldric**, the gentle
monster who's right (10 min) → the first skip, the world tears, *to be continued* (5 min).

> **The closing beat of the demo** is the first skip — the player should finish the hour *certain* this
> is a small story, and be told, in three disorienting seconds, that it is the largest one they've ever
> played. That's the hook that converts a demo-player into a backer.

### Why this slice (production logic)
- It exercises **every system** (creation, combat, exploration, dialogue, companions, the moral
  mechanic, the foreshadow discipline) so technical risk is found *early.*
- It's **emotionally self-contained** — a complete small story with a gut-punch — so it can ship as a
  free demo / festival build that *sells the full game by itself.*
- It reuses content we've **already designed** (`03` Prologue, `10` §1 Aldric, the Cinderhaunt already
  in code, `15` foreshadowing) — minimal net-new design, maximal proof.

## PART III — SCOPE, BUDGET & RISK (the honest part)

### Scope reality
A full BG2-scale CRPG is a multi-year, funded-team effort (BG3: ~400 people, 6 years). **Do not build
the whole thing first.** Build in this order:
1. **Vertical slice** (the hour above) — solo or tiny team, ~3–6 months.
2. **Prologue + Act I, complete** (Baldur's Gate, fully reactive) — the "Episode 1" you could ship.
3. **One time-travel era** (Netheril — the falling city — is the most spectacular hook) as the proof
   that the era-module structure works.
4. **The remaining eras + the finale**, era by era, each a shippable milestone.

### Budget tiers (rough, for planning the ask)
- **🌱 Lean (solo + contract art/music):** the vertical slice + Act I. The "prove it" tier.
- **🌿 Focused (small team, ~5–8):** Acts I–III, two eras, full companions for those acts. The
  "Episode" tier — genuinely shippable, Disco-Elysium-sized ambition.
- **🌳 Full (funded studio):** all five acts, all six eras, full VO, the complete six-ending matrix. The
  dream tier — pursue only after the Focused tier proves the audience.

### The anti-scope-creep rules (from `ROADMAP.md`, restated because they're load-bearing)
1. No new system without a cut elsewhere until the slice ships.
2. Reactivity > breadth: a small world that *remembers* beats a big one that doesn't.
3. **Externalize all strings and keep the flag/clue spreadsheet from day one** (the Foreshadowing Ledger
   *is* a production document, not just a creative one).
4. The slice is sacred. Everything serves the slice until the slice is gripping.

### Top risks & mitigations
| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **IP** — Forgotten Realms is WotC's | Everything is data-driven; the *plot/characters/Unmade are original*; reskin names for commercial release (the 5e rules are SRD-open) |
| **The twist falls flat** | The Foreshadowing Ledger + the replay-test discipline; playtest the slice's seed-planting specifically |
| **Scope kills it** | The era-module structure + "ship the slice first" + the budget tiers above |
| **Tone whiplash** (grim → funny) | Roen/Varra exist *so* the dark lands; the tone bible (`18`) keeps the melancholy-warm consistent |
| **Combat feels like filler** | Lay/Shatter + question-bosses (`19`) make every fight thematic |

### The one-line ask
> *"We've written the 45,000-word bible and designed the twist that makes people gasp. Fund the one
> hour that proves it — and we'll build you the CRPG people will still be talking about in twenty
> years."*
