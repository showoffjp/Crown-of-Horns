# 🎭 Companions — The Full Dossiers

> The deep version. Each of the seven gets a complete treatment: identity & build, full
> backstory, the wound, the recruitment scene, the **personal quest** broken into chapters,
> banter, the **romance arc** (where one exists) beat by beat, approval triggers, and every
> possible fate. Spoiler-complete. (Summaries live in `02_CHARACTERS.md`; this supersedes them.)

> **Design throughline:** every companion is a different *answer* to the game's central
> question — *what is a soul worth that no god ever claimed?* Garrow answers with duty, Roen
> with belonging, Varra with defiance, Naeve with knowledge, Ilfaeril with penance, Maerin
> with refusal, the Mournlight with mercy. Their arcs are those answers being tested to
> destruction and reforged.

---

## 1. SISTER GARROW — *"The Office of the Dead"*
**Full name:** Edda Garrow, Doomguide of Kelemvor. **Build:** Cleric (Grave domain), heavy
armor, mace & shield; healing, *spare the dying*, and the ability to **lay the Unbound** (a
unique reaction that ends an undead's turn). **Joins:** Prologue/Act I. **Romance:** yes.

### Backstory
Edda Garrow grew up the daughter of a Sword Coast gravedigger — the one the plague-villages
sent for when the priests would not come. She learned death as a *craft* before she learned
it as a faith: how a body settles, how grief curdles if a corpse is left wrong, how to dig in
frozen ground. She took the Doomguide grey because it was the only church that talked about
death the way her father's spade did — plainly, without flinching. She trained under **Aldric
Morn**, who was already a legend, and she loved him the way you love a lighthouse: from a
distance, gratefully, certain it would always be there.

When Aldric fell — when he walked into the Fugue after his dead family and came back wrong —
the church gave a quiet order, and most of his students obeyed it: *forget him.* Garrow obeyed.
She has hated herself for it every day since. She did not follow him into heresy; she also did
not stop him, did not warn the families he would recruit, did not do *anything* but keep
digging. She tells herself her restraint was faith. She suspects it was cowardice wearing
faith's grey.

### The wound
*Obedience that felt like betrayal.* Garrow has spent her life being **correct** and is no
longer sure correct and good are the same word. She needs certainty the way a drowning person
needs air, which is exactly why she distrusts it.

### Recruitment
You find her at the city's edge, alone, laying an Unbound child to rest with rites she's not
sure she still believes — because no one else will, and the alternative is leaving a dead
child to wander. She doesn't ask to join you. She asks you a question — *"When you came back
out of the Wall, did it feel like mercy, or like theft?"* — and your answer determines whether
she sees you as a sign or a symptom. Either way she comes, because you are the one person alive
who has *been there* and can tell her if the thing she serves is as monstrous as she fears.

### Personal Quest — *"The Office of the Dead"* (three chapters)
- **Ch. 1 — The Summons (Act II):** Word reaches Garrow that her own mentor — not Aldric, but
  **Brother Halloran**, the gravedigger-priest who taught them both — has risen Unbound near
  the Fields of the Dead, torn loose by a Choir harvest. The man who taught her that the dead
  deserve dignity is now a thing without any.
- **Ch. 2 — The Long Walk (Act III, can be done across time):** Tracking Halloran's wandering
  corpse becomes a tour of every grave Garrow ever dug, each one asking her the same question.
  You learn the church *knew* Halloran would be harvested and let it happen, to bait Aldric.
  Her faith's institution used her teacher as a worm on a hook.
- **Ch. 3 — The Rite or the Mercy (Act IV climax):** You corner Halloran. He is lucid enough,
  in the way the Unbound sometimes are, to *beg* — not for rest, but to be allowed to keep
  walking, because he is **Faithless** too, and rest means the Wall. Garrow must choose: perform
  the Doomguide rite (which sends him *to the Wall*, "correct" and damning), or commit heresy
  and let him wander free (kind and forbidden), or — if you've unlocked Jergal's road — *judge
  him herself,* the first soul she ever weighs by her own conscience instead of her church's law.
  - **Outcomes:** Rite → Garrow's faith hardens into something brittle and sorrowful. Mercy →
    she breaks with the church. Judgment → she becomes the prototype of the rewritten Law, and
    weeps with a relief she's never felt.

### Romance arc
A slow, grave, adult romance built entirely on the theme of *what is sacred*. It does not begin
with attraction; it begins with **trust** — the first time she tells you a doubt she's never
said aloud. The midpoint is a single, devastating line on a battlefield of the dead: *"I have
buried a thousand people and never once let myself believe I'd see any of them again. You're the
first thing in my life that made me want to be wrong."* The climax depends on her quest: if she
chooses Judgment, the romance's final beat is the two of you, at the end, weighing the dead
together, forever, and calling it a life. **If you pursue the Break the Loop ending, Garrow is
the one who learns your true name and gives you the last rite — your own — willingly, weeping,
honoring the soul no god ever claimed.** It is the most quietly shattering scene in the game.

### Approval & fates
- **Gains:** honoring the dead, mercy to the helpless, choosing accountability over comfort,
  admitting doubt. **Loses:** desecration, cruelty, glib nihilism, mocking faith *or* mocking
  doubt.
- **Fates:** First High Doomguide of the rewritten Law (best); a brittle keeper of the old
  faith; a heretic wanderer laying the dead outside any church; or — if she falls — an Unbound
  you must lay yourself, which is the cruelest mirror the game offers.

## 2. ROEN ALLEYWIND — *"The Song That Names You"*
**Full name:** Roen Alleywind (a name he chose; no one knows the one he was born with). **Build:**
Bard (College of Whispers) / Rogue; rapier, hand-crossbow, lockpicks; **inspiration** buffs,
sneak attack, and out-of-combat the party's face/thief. **Joins:** Act I (already built into the
hub). **Romance:** yes.

### Backstory
Roen was an orphan of Baldur's Gate's Outer City — the slums outside the walls, where the
Flaming Fist doesn't bother to patrol and the Guild raises its own. He survived on charm and
quick fingers until the **Harpers** found him at fourteen, and for the first time in his life
something *chose* him. He'd have died for them. He nearly has, a dozen times. The Harpers gave
him a family, a purpose, and a rule he never questioned: *the mission is the love.* You don't get
attached. You get results.

Then they assigned him you. A Returned soul with a hole in it is, by Harper doctrine, a
**security risk** — a crack reality might leak through — and the order, when it became clear what
you were, was not *protect.* It was *use, learn what you can, and remove.* Roen filed three
reports. Then he stopped. He has been lying to the only family he's ever had for weeks, and the
terror of that — not of the villains, of *that* — is why he never, ever stops joking.

### The wound
*Belonging always came with a price tag, and he's about to find out what it costs to refuse the
bill.* Roen needs to let one person all the way in and survive it — the one thing his entire life
trained him never to risk.

### Recruitment
Already in the hub (the green NPC) — but the *real* recruitment is the moment in Act I when you
catch him in a lie and he could talk his way out and chooses, visibly, terrified, not to. *"You
want the truth? The truth is I was sent to bury you, and three weeks ago I started digging the
wrong direction, and I have no idea who I am if I'm not a good little Harper. So. Hi. I'm Roen.
Apparently I'm yours now. Please don't make me regret the worst decision of my life."*

### Personal Quest — *"The Song That Names You"* (three chapters)
- **Ch. 1 — The Audit (Act I/II):** The Harpers notice Roen's gone quiet. A handler — **Maelle**,
  the woman who recruited him, the closest thing he has to a mother — comes to bring him in, or
  bring him down.
- **Ch. 2 — The Purge (Act III):** It's worse than one defection. A mole has rotted a whole
  Harper cell, and Roen's silence made him the prime suspect; the order is purging itself, and
  good people are dying for his choice. He must decide whether to clear his name by handing you
  over, or clear the *cell* by exposing the real mole — at the cost of Maelle, who's been
  protecting him by lying too.
- **Ch. 3 — The Name (Act IV/V):** The title pays off. Roen — who *chose* his name — finally tells
  you the one he was born with, the orphan name he buried, in the same scene where you're trying to
  remember *yours.* Two people who threw away their names, helping each other dig them back up. His
  choice here mirrors yours: hold on to the self he invented, or accept the one he was given.

### Romance arc
The classic banter-to-bedrock arc, done with teeth. Every flirt is deflection until it suddenly
isn't. The turn comes when he stops performing — when, exhausted after the Purge, he says the
unfunniest true thing he knows: *"I've conned everyone I've ever met into thinking I'm fine.
You're the first person I can't lie to, and it's the most naked I've ever felt, and I think
that's what people mean when they say love, and I hate it, and don't you dare stop."* If
romanced and he survives, the ending card is simply: an inn, two chairs, no mission, for the
first time in either of your lives.

### Approval & fates
- **Gains:** wit, loyalty under fire, protecting the powerless, honesty about cowardice.
  **Loses:** sanctimony, cruelty to the weak, selling someone out for advantage.
- **Fates:** Harper spymaster (reformed the order from inside); exile who keeps you company at the
  end; the one who holds your hand in the Break the Loop ending; or, if betrayed, a Harper blade
  in the dark and a single line of regret in the epilogue.

## 3. VARRA — *"The Fine Print"*
**Full name:** Varra Tasforel (the surname is her patron's, branded on her at the pact — she'd
burn it off if she could). **Build:** Warlock (Pact of the Chain / the Undying patron-flavor);
eldritch blasts, hexes, a quasit familiar named **Spite**; gallows humor as a class feature.
**Joins:** Act I. **Romance:** optional, prickly, comic-tragic.

### Backstory
Varra was *sold.* Not metaphorically — at six years old, her parents, drowning in debt to a
death-power's broker, signed her into a pact to clear it. She grew up knowing the exact price of
her own existence, in coin, because she'd seen the receipt. She is funny the way a knife is
sharp: as a survival tool. Every joke is a small refusal to be only what she was traded for.

Her patron has been silent for years — which is worse than loud, because it means she doesn't
know when the bill comes due. And the bill, she's begun to realize, is circling the **Crown of
Horns.** Her patron is a death-power, possibly Myrkul's own agent, and her pact may be a **leash**
the villains can yank at the exact moment you need her most.

### The wound
*She was currency before she was a person.* Varra needs to learn she is worth more than her
price — which is hard, when you've literally seen the number.

### Personal Quest — *"The Fine Print"* (three chapters)
- **Ch. 1 — The Silence Breaks (Act II):** The patron speaks for the first time in years — through
  Spite, through mirrors, through the mouths of the dying. It wants Varra *near the Crown.* She
  realizes she's been steered her whole life.
- **Ch. 2 — The Broker (Act III):** You track the broker who sold child-Varra — now an immortal
  middleman of soul-debts — to learn the pact's exact terms. The horror: there's an out, but it
  requires her to **sell someone else** the way she was sold. Provide a replacement, or void the
  contract by paying its true price herself.
- **Ch. 3 — Voiding the Contract (Act IV):** Varra chooses. Sell a soul (she can be talked into it
  by a cruel player — and it costs her *everything* that made her worth saving); pay the price
  herself (a piece of her life, her power, or her memory); or — the hard, earned option — **prove
  the contract void from its origin**, because a six-year-old cannot consent, and a death-power's
  law, like the Wall, is only a law because no one ever argued it down. The last option ties her
  arc directly to the game's thesis: *unjust law is just a decision waiting to be unmade.*

### Romance arc
Varra's romance is a war of attrition against her own conviction that she's unlovable because
she's *priced.* She'll test you cruelly, push you away, assume the worst — and the romance is
won not by grand gestures but by *staying* through the tests. The payoff line, if you get there:
*"You know the worst thing about being sold? It's not the owning. It's that some part of you
agrees you were worth selling. You've spent a year refusing to agree. I don't know what to do
with that except — apparently — this."* If freed and romanced, her ending is her **burning the
receipt** at last.

### Approval & fates
- **Gains:** audacity, loyalty under pressure, defying authority, gallows humor, refusing to
  abandon people. **Loses:** self-righteousness, leaving someone behind, bowing to power.
- **Fates:** free and insufferable and finally *worth more than her price*; bound forever
  (collected mid-game if you let the leash pull); or hollowed out, having sold another to save
  herself — alive, free, and unrecognizable.

## 4. NAEVE OF THE SEVENTH ENCLAVE — *"Karsus's Last Theorem"*
**Full name:** Naeve, Arcanist of the Seventh Enclave of Netheril (she has no surname; Netherese
of her caste took the city's number). **Build:** Wizard (Netherese High Magic flavor — evocation
+ chronurgy hints); the party's artillery and its lore-engine. **Joins:** Act III (Netheril).
**Romance:** yes — cerebral, two world-enders learning to be carried.

### Backstory
Naeve was a prodigy in a civilization of prodigies — the flying cities of **Netheril**, where
magic was so total that the powerful literally lived in the sky. She helped derive a piece of
the **theorem** that the archmage **Karsus** later weaponized into the single most catastrophic
spell ever cast: an attempt to *substitute himself* for the goddess of magic. Naeve's
mathematics were clean. Karsus's ambition was not. When he cast it, the goddess died to stop
him, the Weave failed for a heartbeat — and every flying city in Netheril, held aloft by magic,
fell out of the sky at once.

You pull Naeve from the rubble of the Seventh Enclave in the instant of the fall. She has lost
her entire world, her people, and — worse for her — her certainty that **knowledge is clean.**
She helped end a world with elegant proofs, and she will never trust elegance again.

### The wound
*Her genius ended a world.* This makes her dangerously sympathetic to the Unmade's logic —
*just undo the mistake* — because she, more than anyone, wants a mistake undone. Her need is to
accept that some mistakes can only be **carried**, not erased; that a person is more than their
worst correct answer.

### Personal Quest — *"Karsus's Last Theorem"* (three chapters)
- **Ch. 1 — The Surviving Pages (Act III):** Fragments of Karsus's full proof survived the fall,
  scattered across the ruin and across *time*. Naeve wants them — to understand, she says. To
  *use*, you suspect.
- **Ch. 2 — The Margin (Act IV):** Assembling the proof, Naeve finds a marginal note in the
  theorem's oldest copy — in **your handwriting**, dated centuries before your birth. She is the
  first companion to say the unspeakable thing aloud: *you are a loop.* The scene reframes her
  arc and yours simultaneously.
- **Ch. 3 — Burn It or Cast It (Act V):** The completed theorem could give the Unmade exactly the
  tool it needs — a clean substitution, a way to *un-write* without the mess. Naeve must choose to
  **burn the last theorem unused** (accepting that her life's masterwork must die so the world can
  live with its mistakes), or **hand it over** (offering the Unmade its victory, out of a grief
  for her own lost world that she can finally, ruinously, indulge).

### Romance arc
Two people who have each ended a world, circling the question of whether they're allowed to be
loved anyway. It's a romance of *arguments* — long, brilliant, late-night debates about
determinism and guilt that slowly stop being about philosophy and start being about the two of
them. The turn: *"I have spent a thousand years certain that the universe is a proof and we are
all just steps in it. Then you did the one thing my mathematics could not predict. I do not have
a word for what you are. I have decided the word is going to be 'mine,' if you'll permit the
imprecision."* If romanced, her best ending has her teaching children arithmetic *that builds*,
the theorem burned, finally forgiven.

### Approval & fates
- **Gains:** curiosity tempered by restraint, intellectual honesty, choosing truth over comfort,
  mercy. **Loses:** willful ignorance, reckless ambition, cruelty to the curious.
- **Fates:** the one who talks the Unmade *down* (she alone can meet its logic on its own terms);
  the one who hands it the theorem and watches a second sky fall, smiling, undone; or a teacher,
  at last at peace.

## 5. ILFAERIL — *"The Verdict"*
**Full name:** Ilfaeril Auvryndar-no-longer (he renounced his house after the vote). **Build:**
Paladin (Oath of Redemption) / Ranger; sword-and-board with radiant smites and a longbow;
**ten thousand years** of guilt as a stat block. **Joins:** Act III (Crown Wars). **Romance:**
no (a deliberate, aching platonic bond — see below).

### Backstory
Ilfaeril was a lord of the elven high courts during the **Crown Wars**, the millennia of
fratricidal conflict that birthed the drow and broke the elven peoples forever. In a council of
vengeance after an atrocity, the lords voted on the fate of an enemy people — not their lives, but
their *souls.* They voted that the enemy's godless dead be **unmade**, denied even the dignity of
a resting place, so that not even their memory would have anywhere to rest. Ilfaeril raised his
hand. The verdict, repeated across other courts, other wars, **hardened over millennia into the
cosmic Law of the Faithless.** He is, in the most literal sense, one of the people who *wrote* the
central atrocity of the setting — by voting yes one terrible afternoon and never voting it down.

He has spent the ten thousand years since as a half-ghost at the edge of the Fugue Plane,
guiding lost souls past the Wall, trying to atone for a thing too large to atone for. He does not
believe atonement is possible. He keeps trying anyway, which is either the noblest or the most
self-destructive thing in the game.

### The wound
*A single raised hand, ten thousand years of consequence.* Ilfaeril needs to learn that atonement
is not the same as self-erasure — that a person who has done an unforgivable thing is still
*allowed to stop paying*, allowed to rest, allowed to be more than their worst vote.

### Personal Quest — *"The Verdict"* (three chapters)
- **Ch. 1 — The Witness (Act III):** Ilfaeril is the living proof of the game's most dangerous
  idea — that the Wall is a *decision*. The Unmade reveres him as its first witness. He fears that
  makes him its instrument, and he's right to.
- **Ch. 2 — The Descendants (Act IV):** You find the souls of the people he damned — still in the
  Wall, still dissolving, ten thousand years later, because his vote never expired. They know his
  name. They have had a very long time to decide what they'd say to him.
- **Ch. 3 — Forgiveness or the Open Door (Act V):** The climax. The damned can **forgive** him
  (and he must accept it, which is harder for him than any punishment), or he can refuse
  forgiveness and instead **hold the Wall open** — physically, at the cost of his own existence —
  so the souls he condemned can flee through to whatever lies beyond. The first is the Oath of
  Redemption fulfilled; the second is martyrdom, and he'll choose it gladly unless you convince
  him his life is worth more than his guilt.

### The bond (not a romance)
Ilfaeril is the game's argument that *not every deep love is romantic.* He becomes, if you let
him, a kind of grandfather to the whole party — ten thousand years of patience, the one who sits
up with the others on the bad nights. His relationship with **Maerin** is the heart of it: the
ancient man who helped build the Wall, and the girl it swallowed, learning to forgive each other
on behalf of everyone who can't. If both survive, their epilogue is shared.

### Approval & fates
- **Gains:** mercy, oaths kept, humility, accepting forgiveness, protecting the condemned.
  **Loses:** cruelty, pride, despair-as-indulgence, vengeance.
- **Fates:** forgiven and finally *at rest*, sword laid down after ten thousand years (best);
  martyred holding the Wall open, the last one through — or not; or lost to despair, walking into
  the Wall himself to join the people he damned, which the game treats as a tragedy, not a triumph.

## 6. MAERIN — *"Not Your Reason"*
**Full name:** Maerin Morn. **Build:** Ranger (Gloom Stalker, undead-touched); dual short-swords
and a shortbow, with a fading luminescence that lets her *step partly out of the world*. **Joins:**
Act II (pulled from the Wall). **Romance:** **no** — by design. She is the fierce platonic heart.

### Backstory
Maerin is the dead daughter of the central villain — but she was a *person* first, and the game
never lets you forget it. Cheerful, godless, sixteen, in love with the world precisely *because*
she didn't think a god stood behind it: every sunrise was a free gift from no one, and she found
that beautiful, not frightening. She died of the **Howling Pox** in the winter that took her
mother too, and because she believed in nothing, she was walled into the Faithless beside Saerin.

Her father's entire crusade is *about* her. Everyone — Aldric, the Choir, even the Unmade —
treats her as a **symbol**: the innocent the Wall damned, the reason to burn the world. You can
pull her out of the Wall in Act II, and the first thing the resurrected girl does is **refuse the
symbol.** She did not die to be a war-cry. She will not be saved at the cost of a city. She is
*furious*, and luminous, and entirely her own.

### The wound
*She died for nothing, by the cosmos's own accounting — and now everyone wants to make her death
mean something, which is its own erasure.* Maerin wants to be a *person*, not a stake. And her
need — the tragedy at the game's core — may be to be allowed to **finish dying on her own terms.**

### Personal Quest — *"Not Your Reason"* (woven through Acts II–V)
- **Ch. 1 — The Refusal (Act II):** Pulled from the Wall, half-dissolved, Maerin confronts what
  she's become and what she's *for.* She forbids you to use her against her father. (You can break
  this promise. She'll know.)
- **Ch. 2 — The Father (Act III–IV):** The most direct line to Aldric's heart. A reunion scene with
  a dozen branches: she can plead, rage, forgive, or coldly tell him that the daughter he's burning
  the world for would rather have *stayed dead* than be his excuse. Her words can **break** Aldric
  (redemption), **harden** him (he decides she's been corrupted by the Wall, becoming Myrkul's
  perfect vessel), or leave him a weeping ruin who proceeds anyway.
- **Ch. 3 — The Choice That Isn't Yours (Act V):** Maerin is fading no matter what; the Wall's
  dissolution doesn't stop outside it, only slows. The climax is the game's hardest gut-punch:
  *you* can fight to **save** her (binding her soul to the world at a terrible cost — and she may
  not thank you), or you can **let her go** on her own terms, or — if you walk Jergal's road — she
  can choose to **return to the Wall willingly**, not to be dissolved but to anchor the rewrite,
  freeing thousands, her death finally *meaning* something *because she chose it.* Her last line in
  that path breaks Aldric, the player, and the entire moral architecture of the game at once.

### Approval & fates
- **Gains:** honesty, treating her as capable, letting her *choose*, refusing to use her.
  **Loses:** condescension, sentimentality, using her against Aldric after promising not to.
- **Fates:** saved and given *one true year*, godless and laughing (bittersweet — she resents the
  cost); released gently on her own terms; returned to the Wall by choice to anchor the rewrite
  (the most devastating "victory"); or — the cruelest card in the game — preserved *forever* in the
  **Deathless Garden** ending: sixteen and smiling and *static*, loved into a padded eternity, never
  allowed to finish her sentence.

## 7. THE MOURNLIGHT — *"The Mercy You Become"*
**Identity:** A defector fragment of the **Last Returned** — a sliver of your own future self that,
against everything, still wants the loop to *break.* **Build:** mirrors the protagonist (it was
you); a "shadow" unit whose kit reshapes to echo your own choices. **Joins:** late Act IV / Act V,
lore-gated (requires `unmade.understood` + the Nameless thread). **Romance:** no — it's *you*.

### Backstory
When the Last Returned looks at its earlier self and feels something other than certainty — a
flicker of the mercy it has spent centuries grinding away — that flicker has nowhere to go. The
Unmade's frozen world has no room for change, and so the flicker is **expelled**, a piece of grief
the deathless avatar could not metabolize. It drifts back along the loop and finds you: the
**Mournlight**, a faint, mournful echo of who you are and who you'll become, and the only entity in
existence that has been on *both* ends of the loop and still chooses the end.

### The wound & the gift
The Mournlight *is* the wound — your ungrieved grief, given a voice at last. It cannot want
anything (the Last Returned burned out wanting), but it can *need*, and what it needs is the thing
the whole game has been about: **an ending.** Recruiting it is the proof that even the monster you
become contains the seed of its own mercy. Mechanically and narratively, it is the key that
unlocks the two best endings — **Jergal's Keyhole** and **Break the Loop** — because only a piece
of the loop that has seen the whole loop can show you how to leave it.

### The role
The Mournlight has no quest of its own; its quest is *you.* In the final act it speaks the lines
the Last Returned can't — not "become me," but "you don't have to." Its existence is the game's
gentlest argument: that identity across time is not a sentence, that the worst version of you is
still *you* enough to want you free.

## Cross-Companion Banter (samples)

> The party reacts to each other, not just to you. A few representative exchanges:

**Varra & Ilfaeril** (defiance meets penance):
> **Varra:** "Ten thousand years of guilt. You know what that is? That's a *hobby*, old man."
> **Ilfaeril:** "And your jokes — what are those?"
> **Varra:** "...Also a hobby. Shut up. I liked you better as a ghost."

**Naeve & Garrow** (knowledge meets faith):
> **Naeve:** "Your god admits he didn't make the Wall and can't unmake it. What, precisely, is he
> *for*?"
> **Garrow:** "For sitting with the dying when the people who love them can't bear to. Not every
> god needs to move mountains, arcanist. Some just need to stay."
> **Naeve:** *(long pause)* "...That is a better answer than I wanted."

**Roen & Maerin** (the orphan who chose his name; the girl who refuses her symbol):
> **Roen:** "We've got a lot in common, kid. Nobody asked either of us who we wanted to be."
> **Maerin:** "You picked 'Roen Alleywind.' I'd have picked 'still alive.'"
> **Roen:** "...Yeah. Fair. Drinks are on me. Metaphorically. You're sixteen and also dead."
> **Maerin:** "Worst chaperone in the Realms."

**The Mournlight & anyone** (it only ever says variations of one thing):
> "I remember this conversation. I remember everyone in it dying. I am trying very hard to be glad
> I'm here for it again instead of just sad. That's the whole job, in the end. Being glad and sad
> at once, and not letting one eat the other."
