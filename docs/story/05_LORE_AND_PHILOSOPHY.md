# 🜏 Lore, Esoterica & Philosophy

> The deep water. The cosmology the game never explains outright; the forbidden books you can find
> and quote; the philosophical debates each faction embodies; the recurring symbols; and a glossary.
> *Nothing here is told to the player directly — it is assembled from fragments, the way truth is.*

---

## I. The Cosmology — three structures and the secret beneath them

### 1. The Wall of the Faithless
When a mortal dies, the soul arrives in the **Fugue Plane** and waits in the **City of Judgment** to be
claimed by its god. The Faithful are led away to their deity's realm. The **False** (who betrayed their
god) are punished. And the **Faithless** — those who served *no* god, from honest doubt to mere
indifference — are mortared, living and aware, into the **Wall of the Faithless**, where they slowly
dissolve into *nothing* over a span no mortal mind can hold.

The cruelty is not the punishment. The cruelty is the *category.* A soul is damned not for evil but for
**unbelief** — for failing to be *owned.* The Wall is older than Kelemvor, older than Myrkul, older than
Jergal who came before them. No god wrote it. It is treated as bedrock, as physics, as simply the way the
multiverse is. **The game's first heresy is to ask: who decided this? — and the answer, found in the Crown
Wars, is that someone *did.***

### 2. The Weave
All magic flows through **the Weave**, the gift and body of the goddess of magic. The Weave has **died and
been reborn**: once at **Karsus's Folly** (−339 DR), when a mortal tried to seize it and the goddess died
to stop him and was reborn; again at the **Spellplague** (1385 DR), when she was murdered and the Weave
collapsed into blue fire. Crucially: **time, in the Realms, is partly a structure of the Weave** — a
deterministic *score* the world plays. Where the Weave is wounded (the Folly, the Plague), time itself
becomes negotiable. This is the in-fiction license for time travel: the Returned, a crack in the Weave's
score, can slip through its wounds.

### 3. The Wheel of the Dead-Gods
The throne of death changes hands. **Jergal** held it first and abdicated from boredom; the **Dead Three**
(Bane, Bhaal, **Myrkul**) seized it; Myrkul fell in the Time of Troubles and **Cyric**, then **Kelemvor**,
took it. The lesson every villain learns and misreads: *the throne of death is a chair a mortal can take.*
Aldric believes it. Myrkul has done it. The Last Returned sits on it. The golden ending's quiet wisdom is
that the chair is not the problem — **the Wall is**, and the chair cannot fix it, only a hand willing to do
the work can.

### THE SECRET — the Unmade
Here is the keystone the cosmology hides: **nothing is the one thing creation cannot make.** Every soul the
Wall "dissolves to nothing" does not cease — it becomes an *unaccounted remainder,* and ten thousand years
of remainders have **accreted** into a single sorrowing awareness below the Fugue: **the Unmade.** It is not
a demon or a god. It is *grief with mass* — the sum of everyone the cosmos discarded, awake, lucid, and
correct that the arithmetic was monstrous. And it is a **closed loop with no first cause** (see §III).

## II. The Esoteric Texts (findable in-world; quotable)

> Fragments the player can recover — in Candlekeep, the Netherese ruins, the Fugue archives. Each reframes
> the truth a little. None is wholly reliable.

**— *The Ledger of the Unwalled*** (Kelemvor's sealed archive; Vayle's terror)
> "Reform attempted, year 1064: the Wall breached at one niche. Result: the Whispering Plague, 40,000 dead.
> Reform attempted, year 1238: a single soul judged by mercy. Result: it claimed it had been judged, and so
> must the others, and so came the war of the screaming undying. *Conclusion of the Synod: the Wall is the
> least of the available atrocities. Keep it. Tell no one why.*"

**— *Karsus's Last Theorem*** (Netheril; Naeve's quest)
> "Divinity is not seized. It is *substituted.* To become the god of a thing is to delete the god that was —
> and to inherit every debt the deleted god owed. Karsus did not fail because he was too weak. He failed
> because he did not read to the end of his own proof. *I read to the end. I burned the page.*"

**— *The Ilfaeril Confession*** (Crown Wars; carved, not written)
> "We did not discover the Wall. We *voted* it — that our enemies' godless dead be unmade, so that even
> their memory would have nowhere to rest. I raised my hand. Ten thousand years I have told myself it became
> law because it was true. It became law because *no one ever voted it down.*"

**— *The Doomsong of Jergal*** (the eldest scribe's only surviving verse)
> "I read the last page first. It is blank. Not because nothing is written there — because *everything* is,
> and the ink is the same color as the paper. I gave away the throne to see if any of you could read it.
> None of you have. *Yet.*"

**— *The Apocrypha of the Unwalled*** (the Choir's gospel; Aldric's marginalia)
> "They tell us death is a mercy and the Wall is its hem. But mercy that asks a price of *belief* is not
> mercy — it is a toll. I have paid every toll my god demanded for forty years. *I am done paying.*"
> *(Aldric's margin, in a shaking hand: "So am I, old friend. So am I.")*

**— *The Niche*** (found on the Wall itself, in your own hand, dated before your birth)
> "If you are reading this, you have come back again. You always do. Stop reading. Put down the pen. The
> only word that ends it is the one you keep forgetting on purpose. *Remember it, and stay gone.*"

## III. The Philosophy — the arguments the game *is*

Each faction is a position; the player's ending is their thesis.

### A. The ethics of the Wall — *belief as a moral prerequisite*
Is an afterlife that damns *unbelief* (not evil) ever just? **Vayle** says: it doesn't matter if it's just;
it matters that the alternatives are worse (consequentialist atrocity-management). **Aldric** says: an
injustice this old is still an injustice, and arithmetic justifies its removal at any cost (revolutionary
utilitarianism). **Jergal** says: the category is the error — judge souls *individually,* by hand, forever
(restorative justice, at unbearable cost). The golden ending sides with Jergal.

### B. Utilitarian atrocity — *Aldric's math*
A few thousand dead now, to save the *infinite* dead forever. The game refuses to strawman this; it makes
you *feel* the math is sound, then makes you hold the bodies. The question it leaves you: **is there a number
of saved souls that justifies a murdered child? And if you say no — what do you say to the children already
in the Wall?**

### C. Free will inside a scripted cosmos — *the variable in the margin*
If the Weave is a deterministic score, are choices real, or just the discovery of the choice you were always
going to make? You — the **unscripted Returned** — are the cosmos's question mark. Every other piece is
predictable to the Unmade; *you* are not. The game's wager is that **free will is real precisely at the
point where you do the thing your past selves never did.** Ending 5 (Break the Loop) is that point made
literal: the one act with no precedent.

### D. Identity across time — *what can change the nature of a soul?*
The Last Returned *is* you — a you that chose differently and became a monster. Is it therefore *you*? Are
you culpable for it? Are you fated to it? The Planescape question, reframed: not "what can change the nature
of a man," but **"if your worst future already exists and remembers being you, are you free to be otherwise?"**
The answer the game offers: yes — but only by an act of refusal so total it costs your existence.

### E. The meaning of an ending — *mortality as gift or curse*
The Unmade believes death is the original cruelty. The Deathless Garden proves the opposite: a world where
nothing ends is a world where nothing *means.* **Change requires loss; meaning requires endings; love
requires the possibility of grief.** The golden and tragic endings both affirm this; the Abolition ending is
the horror of its denial. *To save everything forever is to freeze it dead.*

### F. Grief — *the engine of the whole machine*
Every villain is a griever; the cosmic antagonist is grief incarnate; the protagonist is grief that won't
stay buried, pulling itself back across time. The game's final, gentle thesis: **grief is love with nowhere
to go — and the only thing that ends it is not erasing the loss, but letting it *rest.*** The loop breaks not
when you defeat your grief, but when you finally *grieve* it.

## IV. Recurring Symbols & Motifs
- **The niche on the Wall** — your reserved nothing; recurs each act, a little more formed. The whole game
  is a slow walk toward it; the endings differ in *how* you arrive.
- **The unremembered word** — your true name, spoken in the prologue, withheld until Act V. To remember it is
  to understand everything.
- **Tea** — Aldric's gentleness; offered in Act I, again in his redemption epilogue. Warmth as the mask of
  the unbearable.
- **The nosebleed / the skip** — the Weave's score skipping a beat as the crack in you widens; the physical
  tell of time coming loose.
- **The same smile, forever** — the Deathless Garden's horror; love made static.
- **The blank last page** (Jergal) — the future as unwritten *or* as written in ink the color of the paper;
  determinism vs. freedom, unresolved until your final choice writes on it.
- **Color:** the living world warm and grimy; the Fugue grey and patient; Netheril gold and doomed; the
  Spellplague a wound of blue fire; the Deathless Garden *too* perfect, oversaturated, wrong.

## V. Glossary
- **The Returned** — a soul pulled back from death; you. A crack in the Weave's score.
- **The Unmade** — the accreted nothing of the dissolved Faithless; sapient grief; the true antagonist; a
  closed loop with no beginning; *you.*
- **The Last Returned** — a future you that said yes and became the Unmade's avatar; your hunter and mirror.
- **The First Discarded** — the first Faithless soul ever damned and pulled back; the loop's "origin"; *you,*
  ten thousand years ago.
- **The Wall of the Faithless** — the afterlife's prison for the godless dead; the central injustice.
- **The Crown of Horns** — relic forged from Myrkul's skull; vessel of his residual divinity; the lure.
- **The Choir of the Unwalled** — Aldric's movement; true-believer martyrs who would tear the Wall down.
- **The Doomguides** — Kelemvor's clergy; keepers of death's order; Aldric's former order, Garrow's still.
- **The Mournlight** — a defector fragment of the Last Returned; your grief, choosing at last to want an end.
- **The Keyhole** — Jergal's secret: that the Wall can be *rewritten,* soul by soul, by hand, forever.
- **The Deathless Garden** — the timeline where you said yes; paradise as a held breath; the game's hell.
