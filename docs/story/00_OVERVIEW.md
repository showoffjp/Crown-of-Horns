# 🪞 Overview — Tone, Themes, and the Tropes We Play

## Logline
*A dead soul, dragged back from oblivion by the grief of everyone the gods threw away, must
walk backward through the apocalypses of history to stop a mercy that would unmake the world
— and discovers the monster behind it all has been wearing their own face since before time
began.*

## Elevator pitch
**The Crown of Horns** opens as a tight, grounded Sword Coast mystery — a grieving holy man,
a forbidden relic, a city of dukes and thieves — in the exact register of **Baldur's Gate**.
By the midpoint it has become **Planescape: Torment** asking what a soul is worth. By the end
it is operating at **Mass Effect / Lord of the Rings** scale: a journey across the literal
hinge-points of a world's history to decide whether death itself is a cruelty worth abolishing,
paid for by a protagonist who slowly realizes they are not the hero of the story but its
**origin**. It is a tragedy with the structure of a mystery and the heart of a love letter to
everyone the world forgot.

## Comparables (the bar we are aiming at)
- **Baldur's Gate II:** companion depth, reactive choice, a charismatic antagonist who is
  *right* in ways that hurt, the slow escalation from local to cosmic.
- **Planescape: Torment:** "What can change the nature of a man?" — identity, memory, mortality,
  a protagonist whose past is the central mystery, philosophy as gameplay.
- **Disco Elysium:** ideas treated as characters; the world's history as a wound that thinks.
- **Lord of the Rings:** the weight of deep time; the sorrow inside even the triumphs;
  "the great tales never end."
- **Dark Souls / Hidden lore:** esoteric truth assembled from fragments, never spoon-fed.

## The central question
> **"What is a soul worth, that no god ever claimed?"**

Every system, villain, companion, and ending is an argument about that sentence. The Wall of
the Faithless says: *nothing — and nothing is what you become.* The whole game is the slow,
terrible discovery that *nothing is the one thing the multiverse cannot actually make* — and
what wakes up when you try.

## The themes (the load-bearing ones)
1. **The justice of the gods.** Is an afterlife that punishes *unbelief* justice, or the oldest
   tyranny dressed as law? Can an evil so ancient it looks like physics be torn down without a
   new tyrant taking its place?
2. **Grief as a force of history.** Every villain is a griever. The plot is literally *powered*
   by grief — the Unmade is condensed sorrow. The game asks what grief builds and what it burns.
3. **Free will inside a scripted cosmos.** The Weave is, in a sense, a deterministic score the
   world plays. You — the Returned, the unscripted variable — are the question mark in the
   margin. Can a soul actually *choose*, or only discover the choice it was always going to make?
4. **Identity across time.** If a future version of you made a different choice and became a
   monster, is it *you*? Are you the same soul you were the first time you died? *What can
   change the nature of a Returned soul?*
5. **The meaning of an ending.** Is mortality the curse the Unmade believes, or the only thing
   that makes a life mean anything? The golden ending and the tragic ending split on this.
6. **Restorative vs. retributive justice; utilitarian atrocity.** Aldric's arithmetic — a few
   thousand dead to save the infinite dead — is never strawmanned. The game makes you *feel*
   the math, then makes you feel its cost.

## Emotional spec — the highs and lows we promise
- 🜏 **Mystery:** a slow-assembled truth across eras; nothing important is ever simply *told*.
- 🕯️ **Tragedy & loss:** a companion can die; a daughter is already dead; the kindest ending
  is the one where *you* don't survive it.
- 💞 **Romance:** at least three slow-burn romances, each one a small argument about the themes
  (faith, trust, mortality). Love here is never a reward; it's a stake.
- ⚔️ **Adventure & triumph:** flying cities, god-haunted battlefields, a heist in Candlekeep, a
  descent into the City of Judgment, a duel with your own reflection.
- 🌌 **Existential awe & dread:** standing inside the instant magic dies; hearing a million dead
  speak as one voice; learning the loop has no beginning.
- 🌅 **Catharsis:** every ending, even the darkest, earns its final image.

## Tropes we deliberately use — and how we subvert them
| Trope | Played straight as… | Subverted into… |
|---|---|---|
| **The Chosen One** | "You alone can stop this — you're special, marked, Returned." | You're not chosen to *save* the world; you're the **origin of the threat**. The mark is a wound the villain put in you before history began. |
| **The Dark Lord** | A singular evil to defeat at the end. | **Four nested masks**, each sympathetic; the deepest one's grievance is *just*; the final one is **you**. |
| **The Mentor** | Aldric is wise, paternal, teaches you the world. | He's the **villain**, and his lessons are true — which is the horror. |
| **Time Travel** | Visit the past to fix the present. | A **closed causal loop / bootstrap paradox**: you cause the thing you're trying to stop; your "destiny" is a memory of another self's choices. |
| **The Reflection Boss** | Fight an evil double. | The double **remembers being you** and believes it is *sparing* you the long road to the same throne. It is not wrong that you'll become it. |
| **The Power of Friendship** | Companions save the day. | Companions are arguments you might *lose*; one can leave, one can die, one is the villain riding in your pocket. |
| **Save the World** | Defeat evil, restore order. | The "evil" wants to **end an atrocity**; "saving the world" may mean **preserving the Wall**. There is no clean win — only the cost you can live with. |
| **The Bittersweet Ending** | Win, but pay a price. | The **best** ending costs you your own existence; the **happiest-looking** ending is the most damning. |

## Structure at a glance
**Prologue + five acts.** Local mystery → infiltration & revelation → time-travel into the
first apocalypses → reality-thin late history → convergence in the afterlife. Six endings that
diverge on what you believe a soul, an ending, and a self are worth. See `03_ACTS.md`.

## Content notes
Death, grief, child-death (handled with weight, never exploitatively), suicidal ideation
(Maerin, Bror-type companions, the protagonist's own second death as a *choice*), religious
trauma, and cosmic-horror dread. The tone is melancholic and humane — sorrow with warmth, not
nihilism. There is humor (Roen, Varra) precisely so the dark lands.
