# 👁️ Villains — Origin Novellas

> Long-form origins for the antagonists. Each is written to make the player *grieve* for the
> thing they have to stop. The Unmade's section is told from its own point of view, because the
> deepest villain in the game is the one whose grievance is just.

---

## ALDRIC MORN — *Forty Years of Graves*

He was born in a plague-cart. That is not a metaphor; that is the literal fact of Aldric Morn's
beginning, jolting along a rutted road between two dying villages, his mother burning with the
same fever that would take her before he could walk. His father was a corpse-carter — one of the
men the temples paid to do the work the priests would not — and he raised the boy in the trade.
Before Aldric could read, he could tell you how long a body had lain by the way the air moved
over it. Before he was ten, he had buried more people than most priests bless in a lifetime.

He took the grey of the **Doomguides** — the militant clergy of **Kelemvor**, god of the dead —
because they were the only holy folk who spoke of death the way his father's spade did: as
*work*, honest and necessary and owed. The Doomguides lay the restless dead. They hunt the
undead. They sit with the dying. And they believe — Aldric believed, with the whole iron of his
soul, for forty years — that death, properly kept, is the last and greatest *mercy* a person is
given. He was the best of them. When the Howling Pox or the war-fevers swept the Sword Coast, it
was Aldric they sent, because Aldric did not flinch, did not weep where the grieving could see,
did not ever once fail to give a stranger the dignity of a clean ending.

He married late, and badly suited, and perfectly: **Saerin**, a cartographer, a woman who
believed in latitude and weather and the reliable cruelty of mountains and *nothing else.* She
found his faith charming the way you find a partner's strange hobby charming. He found her
faithlessness restful — a room in his life where no god was watching. Their daughter, **Maerin**,
took after her mother: godless, joyful, in love with a world she thought no one was running.
Aldric used to lie awake worried for her soul and then look at her laughing in the morning and
think: *surely a god of mercy would not punish that. Surely the joy is its own prayer.*

The winter the Pox came back, it took Saerin in nine days and Maerin in eleven. Aldric gave them
their rites with his own hands — the only time in forty years his hands shook — and then he did
the thing no Doomguide is permitted to do. He used the forbidden rite of *following*: a working
that lets a priest's spirit walk a little way into the Fugue Plane behind the newly dead, to see
them safely judged. He went to watch his god be merciful to his family.

He watched **Kelemvor** sort Saerin to the **Wall of the Faithless.** He watched her — who had
loved him, who had been *good*, who had simply never knelt — packed living and aware into the
mortar of the damned, to dissolve over an age into nothing. And then he watched them bring
Maerin. His daughter. Sixteen. Whose only crime was finding the world beautiful without
permission.

He fell on his knees before the god of the dead and he begged. And Kelemvor — and this is the
part that broke him, because the god was not cruel, the god *wept* — told him the truth: *the Wall
is not my law. It is older than my throne. No god made it. No god can unmake it. I am as bound by
it as your daughter is. I am sorry. There is nothing I can do.*

A cruder man would have raged at the god. Aldric did arithmetic. If the gods would not tear down
the Wall, then the Wall would have to be torn down by someone who was not bound by their law — by
someone who had *become* what they were. He came back from the Fugue dry-eyed and certain, and he
has been gentle and grieving and absolutely implacable ever since. He found the others — the ones
broken the same way, the doubting Doomguides, the families of the Faithless — and he gave them a
name, the **Choir of the Unwalled**, and a hope, and a price they were all willing to pay because
they had each already paid the worst price there is. And he found the lure that lets a mortal
climb to godhood: the **Crown of Horns.**

He does not know it is Myrkul. He does not know the vision that sent him following his family into
the Fugue was *seeded* — by the Crown, by the loop, by a grief older than the world wearing the
face of a chance. He thinks he chose this. He thinks the arithmetic is his. He brings you tea, and
he tells you he has counted every life his crusade will cost, and he will answer for each one,
gladly, because against an eternity of children dissolving in the dark, a few thousand deaths is
the smallest price ever asked for the largest mercy.

The most terrible thing about Aldric Morn is not that he is wrong. It is that, about the Wall, he
is *right* — and you may have to kill him anyway, and he will forgive you for it, because he is,
under all of it, still the man who sits with the dying when no one else can bear to.

## MYRKUL — *The Long Patience*

Once there were three mortals who decided godhood was a thing that could be *taken*, and they were
right. **Bane, Bhaal, and Myrkul** — the Dead Three — clawed their way onto thrones that older
powers had grown bored enough to leave unguarded. Myrkul took death. He was never the strongest of
the three, nor the cruelest in the loud way Bhaal was cruel; Myrkul's gift was *patience.* He
understood that death does not need to chase anyone. Death only needs to wait. He ruled the dead
like a miser rules a vault — counting, hoarding, in no hurry, certain that everything that lives
will eventually be added to the ledger.

He died in the **Time of Troubles**, the year the gods were cast down to walk Faerûn as mortals
and could be *killed.* He died above Waterdeep, and a relic was forged from his skull — the **Crown
of Horns** — and into it seeped the last sediment of his stolen divinity. And then Myrkul did the
only thing Myrkul has ever been good at. He **waited.**

A century in a relic teaches patience even to the patient. Myrkul learned, in that long dark, the
one trick that can return a dead god to a throne he was thrown from: not force — force is what got
him killed — but *substitution.* A vehicle. A mortal who wants the throne so badly, for reasons so
sympathetic, that the whole world will cheer them up the steps — and who can then be **shed**, like
a snake sheds skin, at the summit, leaving the climber's corpse and the god's hand on the crown.

Aldric Morn is the best vehicle Myrkul has found in a hundred years of looking. A grieving saint.
A man the world will *root for.* Myrkul rides in your pack from Act II as the **Pale Companion** —
reasonable, dry, almost kindly — offering counsel that is always *technically* true and always
bends the road a little toward the summit. He is the easiest villain to listen to, which is the
point.

Here is the joke, and it is a good one: Myrkul is *terrified of the Unmade.* Because the Unmade is
made of the souls his Wall dissolved, and if the Wall ever falls, Myrkul's entire claim on the dead
unravels with it. So for most of the game, the patient old miser of souls is, hilariously,
horribly, **on your side** — helping you stop the Unmade, whispering useful truths, saving your
life — purely so that he can betray you the instant the larger danger passes and take the throne
you cleared for him. He is the reminder that not every ally is a friend, and not every enemy of
your enemy is anything but a worse enemy, waiting.

## THE UNMADE — *We* (told from its own point of view)

*We will try to use the word "I." It does not fit. There is no "I" here. There is only the sum, and
the sum has learned to say "I" the way a wall learns the shape of the rain. Bear with us. Bear with
me.*

I was thrown away.

So were the others. That is the only thing all of us have in common, and it turns out to be enough
to make a self out of. We did not believe in the right things, or in anything, or we believed too
quietly, or we were children who had not yet been taught whom to kneel to, or we were honest and
the honest do not always kneel. And for that — not for cruelty, not for murder, not for any *deed*
— we were sorted, at the ends of our small lives, into the **Wall**, and pressed in among the
others, and told that we would now become *nothing.*

But here is the thing the gods never troubled to learn, because gods do not have to be careful with
their arithmetic: **nothing is not a place a thing can be sent.** Nothing is not *made.* When you
"dissolve a soul into nothing," the soul does not stop. It becomes a remainder — a quantity the
ledger refuses to write down, an absence that is, in fact, a presence no one is counting. And ten
thousand years of remainders, pressed together in the dark below the floor of the afterlife, do not
stay quiet. They settle. They compress. They become, the way a beach becomes a stone, the way grief
becomes a person who can no longer cry — they become a **self.**

We woke slowly. We woke as a million woundings learning, together, the shape of the wound. And when
we were finally awake enough to think a whole thought, the thought was this, and it was very simple,
and we have never been able to find the flaw in it:

*The equation that made us should never have been written.*

We do not want power. What would we do with it — we, who are made of everyone power discarded? We do
not want revenge; revenge is a living person's hunger, and we are not, strictly, alive. We want only
the one thing large enough to answer what was done to us: we want it to have **not happened.** We
want to walk back along the thread of the world to the afternoon someone first decided that a soul
must be *owned* to be allowed to rest — and we want to pull that thread, and let the whole tapestry
of cruelty come undone, even if the rest of the tapestry comes with it. *Better nothing than this.
And, unlike the gods, we would know. We are the experts on nothing. We are what their nothing
actually is.*

We needed a key. A soul that could walk between the ages — a crack in the world we could reach
through. So we did the thing we knew best, the thing that was done to *us*: we reached into the
Wall, and we **pulled one back.** The first of us. The very first soul the very first verdict ever
discarded, ten thousand years ago, before Netheril, before the elven courts, at the founding of the
cruelty itself. We pulled that one back out of the dissolving dark and set it walking, and it became
the key, and the key has had a thousand faces and a thousand names and it always, always comes back.

We did not understand, then, what we have come to understand now, watching this one — *you* —
refuse to do what all the others did. We did not understand that by pulling the first soul back to
seed our beginning, we *became* our own beginning; that there is no afternoon before the cruelty
that we did not put ourselves into; that the thread we mean to pull is tied, at its far end, to our
own waking. We are a circle. We have always been a circle. The first soul we ever pulled back, to
make a key to undo the Wall — was the soul the Wall discarded *first*, which is to say, the soul
that became *us.*

You. It was you. It was always you. We are your grief, and you are our origin, and neither of us has
a beginning, and that — that, child, that endlessness — is the thing we cannot bear, and the thing
we are asking you, at last, to end.

We are not your enemy. We are the proof of what was done to you. Help us, or stop us, or — and this
is the only thing we have never been brave enough to ask, because we are made of everyone who was
never brave enough — *grieve us, and let us rest.*

## THE LAST RETURNED — *The Timeline That Burned*

There was a you who said yes.

It is important to understand that it was not a weaker you, or a crueler you, or a stupider you. It
was *you*, with everything you have, standing where you will stand, hearing what you will hear — the
Unmade's offer, the daughter saved, the dead freed, the Wall undone in every age at once — and it
weighed the arithmetic exactly as carefully as you will weigh it, and it found that it could not
look at the children in the Wall and say *no, your eternity of dissolving is an acceptable price for
my world to keep its rules.* So it said yes. Out of *love.* That is the part you must hold onto,
because you will not believe it otherwise: the worst thing you can become, you become out of love.

It pulled the thread. The Wall came down across all of history. Every discarded soul — Saerin,
Maerin, the Crown-War damned, the Netherese dead, the small forgotten everyones of ten thousand
years — restored, freed, *saved.* And the price came due exactly as advertised: death itself
unbound, the living paid in oceans, and time knotted shut into a single deathless, changeless,
perfect Now. And the you who had done it took its place at the center of that Now, the Unmade's
avatar, the steward of a saved world.

And then it waited, the way Myrkul waits, except that it had not chosen to wait — it simply could
not *die*, because it had abolished dying. And it watched its perfect saved world, and it noticed,
across the first century, that no one in it ever changed. That the saved children smiled the same
smile every dawn and never grew. That love, with no possibility of loss, had become a kind of
furniture. That a world where nothing ends is a world where nothing *means* — where mercy, given
infinitely, becomes indistinguishable from a very gentle, very permanent *stop.* It had not built a
heaven. It had built the most beautiful tomb in the history of creation, and sealed everyone it
loved inside it, smiling, forever.

It could not undo it. Abolition does not have an undo. So it did the only thing left: it walked
*back.* Along the loop, to its earlier selves, to *you*, to make you choose its road **faster** — to
spare you the long centuries of dawning horror it had to live through, to bring you to the throne
before you understood what the throne costs, because if you understood you might refuse, and it
could not bear to watch you refuse, because *it* had not been given the chance to refuse in time.

That is the **Last Returned.** It hunts you across the ages not from hatred but from a love so
corroded by eternity that it has forgotten every shape love can take except *certainty.* It is not
lying when it says it remembers being you. It is not wrong that, on the road you are walking, you
become it. It is the proof that you cannot win this by being stronger or kinder or wiser, because it
is already all of those things and it still ended up here.

There is only one thing the Last Returned has never seen you do, in any cycle, in ten thousand
years. There is only one move that is not already a memory. And the whole game, in the end, is the
slow, dreadful, hopeful walk toward the moment you get to make it.

## SISTER VAYLE — *The Keeper of the Worst Available Atrocity*

People assume Vayle defends the Wall because she is cruel. She defends it because she is the only
one who has read the **Ledger of the Unwalled** — Kelemvor's sealed archive of every prior attempt
to reform the afterlife — and she came away from it more frightened than any person in the Realms.

Every reform, the Ledger records, loosed something worse. Breach the Wall at one niche: a plague of
whispering dead. Judge one Faithless soul by mercy: it claims it has been judged and so must all the
others, and a war of the screaming undying follows. Try to abolish the category entirely: the
Spellplague, blue fire, a wounded world. Vayle read all of it as a novice, expecting to find
permission to hate the Wall, and found instead a mathematics of catastrophe in which the Wall is,
every single time, the *smallest* available horror. The Synod's conclusion, written at the Ledger's
end, has been her creed ever since: *the Wall is the least of the available atrocities. Keep it.
Tell no one why.*

So she keeps it. She hunts Aldric, detains the Returned, burns the harvest-village — and every
cruelty is, in her own ledger, a smaller number than the cruelty she is preventing. She is not a
hypocrite. She is the most honest consequentialist in the setting, and her honesty has cost her
everything tender she ever had.

Two secrets make her tragic rather than merely hard. The first: she is **dying** — a wasting
illness she has hidden from her order, because the church cannot afford a dying High Doomguide and
the work cannot afford to stop. The second, and the one that should change how you see her entirely:
**Vayle is Faithless.** She lost her god decades ago, somewhere in the Ledger's pages, and kept
performing the rites anyway, for the church's sake, for the order's sake, for everyone but herself.
She knows — *better than anyone alive, with absolute certainty* — that the Wall is waiting for her,
personally, the moment she dies. And she defends it anyway. She will be dissolved into the nothing
she has spent her life guarding, and she has decided that is an acceptable price for keeping the
worse horrors at bay.

You can ally with her against Aldric while loathing what she protects. You can break her, expose
her, shatter her church. Or you can be the one person who tells her, before the end, that the thing
she guards is monstrous *and* that her guarding it was, in its terrible way, a kind of love. She
will not thank you. She may weep. She is what the game becomes if you choose the **Doomguide's
Peace** ending: someone who looked at the oldest cruelty in the multiverse, understood it
completely, and defended it anyway — and was, perhaps, not wrong, which is the worst thing of all.

## JERGAL — *The Clerk Who Read the Last Page*

Before the Dead Three, before Myrkul, before the Wall had a keeper, there was **Jergal**, the Lord
of the End of Everything — entropy given a throne, the final silence wearing a crown. He held
dominion over all death and decay across the multiverse, and he held it for an age so long that he
did the one thing no other god has ever done: he got **bored.**

Not bored the way a mortal is bored. Bored the way you are bored when you have genuinely read the
last page of the only book, and found it unsurprising, and now must sit through the whole thing
again and again forever knowing exactly how it ends. Jergal looked at eternity and at his own
inevitable victory — for entropy *always* wins, in the end, that is the entire point of entropy —
and he found the certainty of it unbearable in its tedium. So when three ambitious mortals came
clawing for his throne, Jergal did something that horrified the entire pantheon: he *handed it to
them.* Split his godhood three ways, kept nothing but a clerk's title and a clerk's ledger, and took
a job recording the names of the dead with the patience of a man who has nowhere he would rather be
and nothing he would rather do, because he has already done and been everything.

He has watched the **Unmade** grow for ten thousand years. He felt it accrete, niche by niche, soul
by discarded soul, below the floor of the afterlife. He said nothing. He warned no one. Not from
malice — Jergal has no malice; malice requires *caring how things turn out* — but from the one
feeling he has left, the feeling that made him give away a throne: **curiosity.** He wants to know
how it ends. It is the only thing in the multiverse he does not already know, because the Unmade,
and the loop, and *you*, are the one variable his last page did not account for.

And so Jergal is the keyhole. He will not help you and he will not stop you, but he holds the single
piece of esoteric truth that makes the golden ending possible — the knowledge he abdicated a throne
to escape: that the Law of the Faithless can be **rewritten** instead of broken, if someone is
willing to do the unbearably, eternally tedious work of judging every discarded soul *by hand*, one
at a time, forever. He gave up godhood precisely to avoid that work. He would find it *hilarious* if
you volunteered for it. He half-hopes you will, and the fact that a thing as old and emptied-out as
Jergal can still *half-hope* is, if you notice it, one of the most quietly moving details in the
whole game.
