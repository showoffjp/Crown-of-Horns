v6.24.0 · THE LONG TABLE — the room behind all the doors, and the saga's beating heart. A door in Sigil almost no one finds leads not to a world but behind them all, to a long table scarred with the rings of a thousand cups and the small bitten edges where carved things were rolled and rolled again. The Keeper who has been telling reveals the truth under the whole saga: the Wall — every world's Wall — is where the loved-and-set-aside go. Not the unloved. The unfinished. The hero whose story stopped mid-quest; the companion left forever at a cold campfire; the whole worlds dreamed at this table and abandoned when the circle broke, when the tellers grew up and grew apart and the candles burned down and no one came back to light them. And the cure is the thing this entire game has secretly been about: someone, years on, stops and says "do you remember?" — and the remembering reaches down into the stone and pulls the soul home. The Returned didn't cheat death. It was remembered hard enough to come back. Speak the names. A love letter not to D&D the rules, but to the table, the circle, the friends, and the decades. Nothing imagined with love is ever truly gone — it only waits, for someone to come back and say "now, where were we?"
v6.23.0 · THE HEARTH OF FAERÛN — Sigil grows its first door: a homesick Faerûnian taphouse where the Realms' own legends gather, because even the greatest heroes of one prime are just expats in the City of Doors. A love-letter cast: VOLO (Volothamp Geddarm) — the chaotic-neutral loremaster, raconteur and tease who's been everywhere/everywhen and survives on pure luck, recruitable as your guide across the doors; ELMINSTER, the thousand-year Sage who admits he doesn't know what a soul is worth — and that "understanding" was his own coward's flinch; GORION, the Harper who died a father, not a hero, and needs to know the love he poured into a marked child held; DRIZZT DO'URDEN, who refused his birth and (with the Companions) came back from death — kin to the Returned; and JON IRENICUS, the Shattered One at the cold threshold, the saga's hardest case: a soul severed from the gods who chose the monster — reach the Joneleth under him (a Persuasion check, crit/fumble), or judge and turn away; and CADDERLY BONADUCE, the doubting skeptic who served before he believed and ascended toward godhood — the perfect indictment of the Wall: a worthy soul earns the claim, the claim comes last, and the godless faithful were passing the test without being told they were graded. Each a Realms answer to: what is a soul worth that no power claimed?

The Causeway — Thirty-Six Walkable Zones (a saga + side quests)
Sixteen whole walkable zones in one file — a complete saga from a cold market to the throne of the dead and the years after, plus self-contained side quests — walk a character around an isometric market square (click to move, just like the combat sim) and meet seven named souls (Sable the charm-seller, Bram the recruiter, Pip the street-child, Brother Calix the doubting Doomguide, Wren the harvest-marked, Goodman Tallow the Choir's watcher, Mad Joss the fountain-prophet) — then cross a glowing causeway tile to the riverside Reed-Walk (Old Cassian the boatman, Sister Vharn the soul-measurer, the Reed-Wife drowned four centuries, and Wren again at the water's edge) — or take the stair down to the Underbridge, where Pip's dying little brother Wick lies on a pallet, Pip keeps a six-day watch, and the Knotwife — a blind weaver of bridge-banners — holds the name woven into the crossing itself. What you do in one zone follows you to the others: save Wren and she greets you at the river; sell her out and Sister Vharn thanks you by name; help Pip in the market and she lets you near her brother; learn at the river that the knot has a name and the Knotwife will finally speak the four-centuries-buried truth — then climb the causeway past the last torch to the Wall of the Faithless itself, where Brother Hale slowly freezes at his post, Goodwin waits cheerfully for a ferry that will never come, and a soul in the stone opens her eyes: Esuele, the Knotwife's lost daughter — tell her her blind mother still weaves her name every day, and watch four centuries lift. Brave the Wall's cold to fetch the greywort that saves the dying boy under the bridge — then climb back up into the living city, the Lamplit Quarter, where Mab the tavern-keep gossips about everything you've done across all five zones, Pell the Choir's ledger-clerk quietly writes down the marked, and Dace Iron, a Faithless blade-for-hire, can be recruited as your first companion — with her own approval score and a hook that reads whether you shook the Wall or sold a girl to the Choir. Finally, go deeper in to the Counting-House — the Choir's seat — for the Act-1 climax: free Old Crake the gravedigger from his cell, confront Goodman Tallow the smiling man on his own ground, and face Canon Mereth Vael, High Measurer of the Causeway, in a showdown that reads everything — expose the four-centuries-dead clerk who built the Wall, prove it flinched for you, name her line-items back to her, bring a sworn sword, or out-audit her own ledger until it betrays her — then choose how the machine ends (force the souls' appeal, cut Venn's signature, or walk it unfinished together). Finally, make camp at the Hearth — a quiet denouement where the souls who gather at your fire are exactly the ones you saved: recruit Dace and she opens up off-watch; talk Wren off the water and she's there eating a stolen apple; cure Wick and Pip sleeps with both eyes for the first time in years; recruit Sennet and the cartographer maps the camp instead of the afterlife, the cold finally backing out of their hands. Save no one, and the fire burns alone. And when the local Choir lies cracked, a herald brings a summons to the Doomguide's Table — tea with Aldric Morn, the man who watched the Wall take his godless daughter and now means to depose the god of the dead and tear it down, no matter who he must burn. His confrontation reads your entire Act; he reveals the Crown of Horns and asks you to walk into death and back. And beside him, in a chair only your dead-touched eyes can see, sits Maerin — his daughter, who followed him home from the Wall, who has waited twenty years for a mouth he could hear, whose only wish is that he stop. And on the road past camp stands the Grey Wayshrine, where the Judge of the Dead himself sends an aspect to weigh the one soul his ledger can't hold — and here, at last, the saga's deepest secret is spoken: the Wall is no prison but a seawall, the Faithless its mortar, holding back the Hunger (the nothing of every unwitnessed soul) under a Dawn-age Concord the Judge himself hates but is bound to keep. Answer what you'll do about the Hunger when you open the Wall, and a god decides whether to bless you, stop you, or step aside. And at the road's very end stands the Threshold of the Crown's Road, where the living can't follow — and waiting there is the Last Returned: you, from a future that already took the Crown and spent two hundred years freezing into the very thing it broke the world to destroy, crawled back through the crack in time to make you answer the one question it didn't. Here the saga's deepest twist is spoken — the masks are the same soul, looping — and the companions who followed you (Dace, Orin) stand at your shoulder as the variable your future self never had. Break the loop, and lay your worst self to rest. And for the wanderer who strays, a surreal Night Market only the dead-touched can find, where vendors deal not in coin but in years, memories, and names — trade away the grey decades you'd never live for power now (a real barter/shop loop), and meet the souls who came to browse and *never left.* And beneath the Underbridge's old foundations waits the Vault of Tens — a riddle-chamber guarded by a bored bronze head where wit beats correctness (give the "right" answer and it petrifies you; give a clever-wrong one and it *laughs* and lets you through), and where the real solve is realising the vault doesn't guard gold — it guards a caged person named Sela, slowly forgetting her own name, whom a thousand clever souls walked past to grab the hoard. And at the very end, through the death-door, the Court of the Dead itself — where Kelemvor, the Judge, sits bound to a Wall he hates and has waited an age for the one soul who could end it without becoming it; where the Crown of Horns turns at the foot of the throne and tempts you in your own voice (refuse it by naming your own weakness, not your humility); and where the choice you've assembled across thirteen zones finally lands — rebuild the Wall out of the witnessed, force the souls' lawful appeal, relieve the tired god of his throne, or take the Crown and become the next tyrant. The saga reads everything you did — and then, in "Some Years After," a chronicler tells you how the whole world turned out (a tale that changes completely by your ending), while the souls who survived — Wren, old now and warm, her mark thawing; Pip, grown, keeping a roof over the causeway's lost children — appear to tell you, themselves, what your small mercies *became.* And off the Lamplit Quarter, a side quest proper: the Weeping House, a classic CRPG haunting where a family of the dead can't move on, and only you — who sees the dead — can investigate why (a child sees the truth the adults won't; a housekeeper guards it out of loyalty; an Investigation check reads the latched hearth-guard), gather the clues, and confront the grieving Lady with the one fact that frees them all. And beneath the bridge, a monster-with-a-reason: the Old Cistern, where a thing is eating a neighbourhood's *memories* — and it turns out to be a fragment of the saga's own Hunger, a pauper named Nettie forgotten so completely she curdled into a monster. Learn her name from the one old man stubborn enough to still hold it, and you can witness her back into a person — or, if you're too late or too cold, put her down with regret. And down a stair from the Counting-House, a courtroom proper — the Reckoning Court, where the Choir holds the summary trials by which it condemns the Faithless: tonight it means to wall up Goodwife Annet, a godless midwife who caught half the city's children, for the crime of having served no god — and you can stand as her advocate. Argue the granite prosecutor Brother Sevard down (Persuasion, or an Insight that the charge names a state of being, not a crime), play the evidence you gathered upstairs (the forged founding Concord; the High Measurer's cracked faith), intimidate him with a mob of grateful mothers, or — as the only soul who ever walked back out of the Wall — give sworn testimony to what "forfeiture" actually does to a waking soul; then move the bought, weary Reeve Oddleif to be the magistrate he was before the Choir, and watch an old machine hand back its humanity. And down a dusty side-path off the Grey Wayshrine, a god's bargain: the Bone Scrivener — Jergal, the First Lord of the Dead, who held Kelemvor's office aeons before him and gave the whole grim portfolio away out of boredom so total it hollows a deity — now keeps a clerk's desk at the crossroads, and he is the one being who can un-write a soul from the Wall's ledger entirely. But the book must sum to zero, so the price is a piece of yourself — a year of your life, a memory surrendered, or the heaviest coin, a name (your own, the one the harvest already half-took) — and a wavering father and a soul who already paid (a nameless wisp who gave up its own name to save a child) show you exactly what that costs. Or, if you've grasped that the ledger already owes you — a soul spent and returned is money the universe owes itself — you can out-account the inventor of accounting and strike the soul free for nothing but wit (and a nat-20 turns the whole Wall into a vault of uncollected debts the dust will spend an eternity gleefully emptying). And off the Hearth road, a lantern in the dark marks the Mapmaker's Wagon, where Sennet, Cartographer of the Dead, is drawing the first true atlas of the afterlife — survey-grade, taken at the edges, one lost year of warmth at a time — and it is killing them. Their apprentice Tibb counts the days; a soft-spoken buyer, Goodman Vael, has circled the wagon for a fortnight, wanting not a comfort-copy but the working door-map — and only the Returned can see he's a grieving widower chasing a door to his dead wife, whom you can route (a nat-20 names the one thing his patience can't outlast: she'd have hated it) or watch dig in. And because you have walked the blank middle of Sennet's map, you can offer the one thing a cartographer of the dead has never had — a surveyor who goes in and comes out — and recruit them as a third companion, finishing their life's work without it finishing them. Deep, character-reactive talk that reads your race, faith, alignment, background & stats, with nat-20/nat-1 crit comedy, hover-lore, the dead-touched Returned-sense, a Pillars-style reckoning of dispositions, and NPCs that appear on world-state.
