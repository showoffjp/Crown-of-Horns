The Causeway — Nine Walkable Zones (a full Act)
Nine whole walkable zones in one file, an entire Act-1 hub — walk a character around an isometric market square (click to move, just like the combat sim) and meet seven named souls (Sable the charm-seller, Bram the recruiter, Pip the street-child, Brother Calix the doubting Doomguide, Wren the harvest-marked, Goodman Tallow the Choir's watcher, Mad Joss the fountain-prophet) — then cross a glowing causeway tile to the riverside Reed-Walk (Old Cassian the boatman, Sister Vharn the soul-measurer, the Reed-Wife drowned four centuries, and Wren again at the water's edge) — or take the stair down to the Underbridge, where Pip's dying little brother Wick lies on a pallet, Pip keeps a six-day watch, and the Knotwife — a blind weaver of bridge-banners — holds the name woven into the crossing itself. What you do in one zone follows you to the others: save Wren and she greets you at the river; sell her out and Sister Vharn thanks you by name; help Pip in the market and she lets you near her brother; learn at the river that the knot has a name and the Knotwife will finally speak the four-centuries-buried truth — then climb the causeway past the last torch to the Wall of the Faithless itself, where Brother Hale slowly freezes at his post, Goodwin waits cheerfully for a ferry that will never come, and a soul in the stone opens her eyes: Esuele, the Knotwife's lost daughter — tell her her blind mother still weaves her name every day, and watch four centuries lift. Brave the Wall's cold to fetch the greywort that saves the dying boy under the bridge — then climb back up into the living city, the Lamplit Quarter, where Mab the tavern-keep gossips about everything you've done across all five zones, Pell the Choir's ledger-clerk quietly writes down the marked, and Dace Iron, a Faithless blade-for-hire, can be recruited as your first companion — with her own approval score and a hook that reads whether you shook the Wall or sold a girl to the Choir. Finally, go deeper in to the Counting-House — the Choir's seat — for the Act-1 climax: free Old Crake the gravedigger from his cell, confront Goodman Tallow the smiling man on his own ground, and face Canon Mereth Vael, High Measurer of the Causeway, in a showdown that reads everything — expose the four-centuries-dead clerk who built the Wall, prove it flinched for you, name her line-items back to her, bring a sworn sword, or out-audit her own ledger until it betrays her — then choose how the machine ends (force the souls' appeal, cut Venn's signature, or walk it unfinished together). Finally, make camp at the Hearth — a quiet denouement where the souls who gather at your fire are exactly the ones you saved: recruit Dace and she opens up off-watch; talk Wren off the water and she's there eating a stolen apple; cure Wick and Pip sleeps with both eyes for the first time in years. Save no one, and the fire burns alone. And when the local Choir lies cracked, a herald brings a summons to the Doomguide's Table — tea with Aldric Morn, the man who watched the Wall take his godless daughter and now means to depose the god of the dead and tear it down, no matter who he must burn. His confrontation reads your entire Act; he reveals the Crown of Horns and asks you to walk into death and back. And beside him, in a chair only your dead-touched eyes can see, sits Eithne — his daughter, who followed him home from the Wall, who has waited twenty years for a mouth he could hear, whose only wish is that he stop. And on the road past camp stands the Grey Wayshrine, where the Judge of the Dead himself sends an aspect to weigh the one soul his ledger can't hold — and here, at last, the saga's deepest secret is spoken: the Wall is no prison but a seawall, the Faithless its mortar, holding back the Hunger (the nothing of every unwitnessed soul) under a Dawn-age Concord the Judge himself hates but is bound to keep. Answer what you'll do about the Hunger when you open the Wall, and a god decides whether to bless you, stop you, or step aside. Deep, character-reactive talk that reads your race, faith, alignment, background & stats, with nat-20/nat-1 crit comedy, hover-lore, the dead-touched Returned-sense, a Pillars-style reckoning of dispositions, and NPCs that appear on world-state.
